\begin{abstract}
\noindent Two-way fixed-effects (TWFE) difference-in-differences has been the
default estimator in applied health-policy research for decades, yet recent
theory shows it can implicitly weight cohort--time effects in ways that conflict
with the causal parameter. We ask whether the conclusions of 56 published
health-economics papers that used TWFE survive reestimation with modern
heterogeneity-robust estimators (Callaway--Sant'Anna, Sun--Abraham,
Borusyak--Jaravel--Spiess, Goodman-Bacon, and the Rambachan--Roth HonestDiD
sensitivity framework). We replace only the estimator and keep each paper's
original specification. The sign of the effect is preserved in 48 of the 56
cases and reverses in 8. The costs of legacy TWFE designs in
this literature are concentrated in precision and magnitude rather than in
directional conclusions: standard errors rise by roughly 42\% on average, 12
articles lose conventional statistical significance, and the point estimate
moves by more than 20\% in the majority of staggered-adoption designs. Bacon
decompositions show that treated-versus-treated comparisons account for a
substantial share of the identifying variation under staggered TWFE, consistent
with the mechanism emphasized by the theory literature. HonestDiD sensitivity
further shows that robustness to parallel-trends violations is limited under
both TWFE and CS-DID. We release a reproducible metadata-driven pipeline and
the regenerated results for the 56 papers under CC-BY-4.0, compliant with the
AEA Data and Code Availability Policy 1.0.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} difference-in-differences, TWFE, heterogeneous
treatment effects, Callaway--Sant'Anna, Sun--Abraham, Borusyak--Jaravel--Spiess,
HonestDiD, replication, health economics.

\smallskip
\noindent\textbf{JEL codes:} C21, C23, I18.
\end{abstract}

\\section{Introduction}
\label{Chapter1}

Welfare and cost-effectiveness calculations in health-policy research depend on the magnitude of difference-in-differences (DiD) estimates and not only on their sign \parencite{hendren2020unified,finkelstein2020welfare}. Estimator choice can move the implied policy value. The default estimator in this literature remains two-way fixed-effects (TWFE) DiD \parencite{angrist2010credibility}. A decade of methodological work has shown that TWFE combines cohort-time contrasts in ways the researcher did not intend and can return coefficients that resemble causal parameters without estimating one \parencite{goodman2021difference,dechaisemartin2020twfe,callaway2021difference,roth2023s}.

How often does replacing TWFE with a modern heterogeneity-robust estimator change the conclusions of a published DiD study, and along what margin? We re-estimate the headline result of 53 published health-economics DiD studies under a standardized modern-estimator protocol. Sign is preserved in most cases. Of the 53 estimates, 46 (86.8\%) preserve the sign and 7 (13.2\%) reverse it. The dominant pattern is in magnitude. Point estimates shift by more than 50\% in 39.6\% of cases and by more than 100\% in 24.5\%.

The next question follows. Which design margins generate the gap in magnitude between TWFE and modern estimators? Three findings organize the answer.

First, negative weights are second-order in this sample. They appear in most staggered designs and are typically small in absolute terms. The larger sources of divergence are control-group choice, covariate handling, dynamic aggregation, endpoint binning, and software implementation. The practical risk in this literature comes from a set of design choices that affect both the estimand and its implementation, no single one of which dominates.

Second, robustness to treatment-effect heterogeneity does not imply robustness to parallel-trends violations. A heterogeneity-robust estimator sharpens the mapping from the coefficient to an estimand. It does not rescue a design whose identifying counterfactual is fragile. The applied literature still often conflates the two margins.

Third, the patterns in the reanalysis support a short pre-submission checklist and a design-triage decision tree for applied researchers. Box~4.1 lists fifteen specification and reporting choices that most often change conclusions between TWFE and modern estimators. Figure~\ref{fig:decision_tree} guides the choice of the primary modern specification and the sensitivity margins that belong in the main text.

Prior exercises in finance and accounting \parencite{baker2022much}, political science \parencite{chiu2026causal}, and cross-field reproducibility \parencite{brodeur2026reproducibility} document how often TWFE and modern estimators diverge across fields. We decompose that divergence within one field and isolate the role of several design margins, including binning, geographic conditioning, control-group choice, covariate handling, and software defaults. To our knowledge, this is the first applied reanalysis to document their joint contribution in a published DiD literature and to show that negative weights become second-order once these margins are made visible. Health economics is a natural setting. DiD is a workhorse design in the field \parencite{goldsmith2024tracking}, and welfare and cost-effectiveness calculations depend directly on the magnitude of the estimated treatment effect.

This dissertation does not re-collect data, re-identify the ATT from first principles, cover continuous or multi-valued treatments, or claim a census of the DiD literature. It re-estimates a defined sample under a common protocol and reports the patterns that emerge. The remainder is organized as follows. Chapter~\ref{Chapter2} reviews the methodological and reanalysis literatures. Chapter~\ref{Chapter3} describes the sample, protocol, and AI-assisted workflow. Chapter~\ref{Chapter4} presents the empirical lessons and derives Box~4.1 and Figure~\ref{fig:decision_tree}. Chapter~\ref{Chapter5} concludes. Appendices~\ref{AppendixA} through~\ref{AppendixE} report the specification-selection protocol, the per-article results with HonestDiD and Bacon diagnostics, the AI-assisted pipeline, the sample-construction log, and the notation.

\\section{Related Literature}
\label{Chapter2}

\\subsection{Classical TWFE approach and its problems}

DiD is a workhorse design in health economics, and its use has expanded over the past decade \parencite{goldsmith2024tracking}. Corporate finance \parencite{baker2022much}, political science \parencite{chiu2026causal}, and economics more broadly \parencite{brodeur2026reproducibility} have already been reassessed under modern heterogeneity-robust estimators. Health economics has not. This chapter assembles the methodological tools and the reanalysis literature needed to ask the same question in health, with emphasis on the design margins that Chapter~\ref{Chapter4} documents. With multiple periods, staggered adoption, and heterogeneous treatment effects, the coefficient from a standard TWFE regression no longer maps cleanly into the canonical \(2\times 2\) DiD estimand. The problem extends beyond weighting. It also involves the comparisons that identify the effect, the interpretation of dynamic specifications, the role of covariates, and implementation \parencite{dechaisemartin2020twfe, callaway2021difference, sun2021estimating, borusyak2023did_imputation, wooldridge2025two}. In this reanalysis, divergence between TWFE and modern estimators rarely traces to a single margin.

In its most common form, DiD with TWFE is implemented as a linear regression with unit and period fixed effects,
\begin{equation}
Y_{it} = \alpha_i + \lambda_t + \beta D_{it} + \varepsilon_{it},
\label{eq:twfe_static}
\end{equation}
where \(Y_{it}\) is the outcome, \(\alpha_i\) and \(\lambda_t\) are unit and period fixed effects, and \(D_{it}\) indicates treatment. In the \(2\times 2\) case, under parallel trends and no anticipation, \(\beta\) coincides with the usual DiD estimand. In staggered settings, that interpretation breaks down. \textcite{roth2023s} synthesize the recent literature around three issues: variation in treatment timing across multiple periods, potential violations of parallel trends, and alternative frameworks for inference. The discussion below follows that map, with emphasis on the margins that account for the divergence documented in the reanalysis.

\\subsubsection{Weighting, contamination, and the limits of TWFE}

In applied work, a TWFE coefficient in a staggered-adoption setting is often read as an average of cohort-specific effects. That reading is incomplete. \textcite{goodman2021difference} shows that the coefficient is built from a set of \textit{treated-versus-never-treated} and \textit{earlier-treated-versus-later-treated} \(2\times 2\) comparisons. \textcite{dechaisemartin2020twfe} extend the concern by showing, in a broader TWFE setting, that the estimand can be expressed as a weighted average of group-period treatment effects with weights that may be negative. Once treatment effects vary across cohorts or over exposure time, these underlying comparisons stop reading as clean causal contrasts. A single coefficient then hides heterogeneity across the pieces that generate it.

The same problem carries over to dynamic specifications. In event-study regressions,
\begin{equation}
Y_{it}
=
\alpha_i + \lambda_t
+ \sum_{\ell \neq -1} \mu_{\ell}\mathbf{1}\{t-E_i=\ell\}
+ u_{it},
\label{eq:twfe_event_study_short}
\end{equation}
where \(E_i\) denotes the treatment adoption period and \(\ell=t-E_i\) is event time, a common interpretation is that \(\mu_\ell\) measures the average treatment effect at horizon \(\ell\). \textcite{sun2021estimating} show that this interpretation also breaks down under staggered adoption with heterogeneous effects across cohorts. The coefficient for horizon \(\ell\) can load on effects from other relative periods, so the estimated event-study profile may misstate timing, persistence, and pre-trends. Their interaction-weighted estimator averages cohort-specific effects only across cohorts that identify the same horizon.

This literature shows why TWFE can be hard to interpret in staggered settings, in both static regressions and dynamic profiles. It does not show which of these margins matters most in applied work. That ranking is one of the empirical questions of this dissertation. In the 53-article sample reanalyzed in Chapter~\ref{Chapter4}, negative weights appear in most staggered designs but are typically small in magnitude, and most of the TWFE-vs-modern divergence comes from comparison structure rather than from the sign of the weights. If the comparison structure of TWFE is opaque in staggered settings, the next question is whether estimators exist that make that structure explicit.


\\subsubsection{Modern DiD estimators and identification before aggregation}

A common response is that adopting a heterogeneity-robust estimator resolves these problems. The recent literature shows that these estimators relocate the problem rather than remove it. They make explicit the decisions about control group, aggregation scheme, and reference period that TWFE leaves implicit, and they identify treatment effects at the group-time level before any aggregation. In \textcite{callaway2021difference}, the basic object is the group-time average treatment effect,
\begin{equation}
ATT(g,t)
=
\mathbb{E}\!\left[ Y_t(g)-Y_t(0)\mid G_g=1 \right],
\label{eq:att_gt_short}
\end{equation}
where \(G_g\) denotes the group first treated in period \(g\). Treatment effects are defined for each \((g,t)\) cell and aggregated only afterward. The comparison group is explicit from the start. In practice, the main choice is between \emph{never-treated} and \emph{not-yet-treated} controls, which correspond to different versions of the parallel trends assumption. \textcite{marcus2021role} formalize the trade-off. Not-yet-treated controls are often larger and more efficient, but they require a stronger assumption. Never-treated controls rely on a weaker assumption, but they can be costly when the untreated pool is thin.

Related estimators address the same interpretability problem through different routes. \textcite{borusyak2024revisiting} propose an imputation approach that models untreated outcomes and then infers treatment effects. \textcite{gardner2022two} implements a related idea as a two-stage estimator. \textcite{wooldridge2021two,wooldridge2025two} show that a saturated regression with cohort and period interactions, the Extended Two-Way Fixed Effects estimator, recovers the \(ATT(g,t)\) targets of Callaway and Sant'Anna. Divergence between TWFE and modern estimators does not come from weighting alone. It also reflects differences in comparison groups, aggregation rules, and counterfactual construction. With the estimand declared at the \((g,t)\) level, the identification burden returns to parallel trends in a form that can be interrogated directly.

\\subsubsection{Parallel trends and sensitivity}

Even with more interpretable estimators, identification rests on some version of parallel trends. In applied work, this assumption is usually assessed through pre-trend tests in dynamic specifications, with the convention that failure to reject licenses proceeding. That convention is fragile. \textcite{roth2022pretest} shows why the approach is weak. Failure to reject pre-trends may reflect low power, and conditioning on passing the test introduces pre-test bias. \textcite{ghanem2024selection} sharpen the point by linking parallel trends to selection models and to restrictions on unobserved heterogeneity.

\textcite{rambachan2023more} propose a different approach. They replace the pass-or-fail logic of pre-trend tests with explicit sensitivity analysis. Instead of treating insignificant leads as evidence for exact parallel trends, they ask how conclusions change under bounded departures from that benchmark. Robustness to treatment-effect heterogeneity and robustness to deviations from parallel trends are different questions, and the reanalysis treats them separately. Chapter~\ref{Chapter4} reports HonestDiD breakdown values that often lie below pre-trend magnitudes already visible in the event-study data. Heterogeneity and parallel trends are dimensions of identification. Covariates alter the estimand itself.

\\subsubsection{Covariates as part of the design}

Researchers often add covariates linearly to a TWFE regression and treat that step as a generic way to make identification more credible. In DiD settings, that interpretation is too loose. Adding controls changes the identifying assumption from unconditional to conditional parallel trends, so the estimand depends on which covariates enter and how. Including controls in a linear TWFE specification does not guarantee that the resulting coefficient matches the conditional treatment effect targeted by modern DiD designs. \textcite{callaway2021difference} make this explicit. There, \(ATT(g,t)\) is identified after conditioning on pre-treatment covariates, and the choice between never-treated and not-yet-treated controls remains part of the design. This differs from the usual practice of adding controls to a TWFE regression and reading the result as a more flexible version of the same estimand.

The problem sharpens with time-varying covariates. \textcite{caetano2022difference} show that TWFE with controls can fail when covariates are themselves affected by treatment, when identification depends on restrictive functional-form assumptions, or when treatment effects vary with observables. \textcite{caetano_callaway2024_hiddenlinearity} show that, even in two periods, TWFE with covariates can suffer from hidden linearity bias after the fixed-effects transformation. \textcite{wooldridge2025two} reaches a related conclusion in a more flexible staggered-DiD setup, where controls enter with cohort indicators, time indicators, and their interactions. Adding controls to a restrictive TWFE specification does not generally recover the same conditional estimand.

This margin is central in the reanalysis. TWFE with controls is often closer to CS-DID without controls than to CS-DID with controls. The same pattern appears with geographic controls. As geographic interactions become more granular, estimated effects can move sharply. Covariate handling is one of the main channels behind the divergence documented later in the dissertation. These four methodological margins define the state of the literature. The empirical question is which of them move results in applied work.

\\subsection{The reanalysis movement in economics and adjacent fields}

This dissertation belongs to a broader literature that revisits published findings with more credible empirical tools. The reproducibility projects in psychology, especially \textcite{openscience2015reproducibility} and \textcite{camerer2018evaluating}, changed how applied economists read the empirical record, and specification-robustness exercises now form part of that horizon \parencite{vivalt2020specification}. We adopt that perspective and ask how a published set of DiD results changes when the estimator is replaced.

Within economics and adjacent fields, three recent studies are most relevant. \textcite{baker2022much} connect the theoretical critique of staggered TWFE to applications in corporate finance and accounting. Replacing TWFE with heterogeneity-robust estimators changes magnitudes, dynamic profiles, and the interpretation of staggered-adoption designs. Their contribution is to establish the estimator-swap exercise as a unit of analysis. They do not answer whether the patterns recovered in corporate finance transfer to a field with shorter panels, heavier reliance on covariates, and more explicit welfare and cost-effectiveness calculations.

\textcite{chiu2026causal} advance in the same direction in political science. They reanalyze 49 studies published in the \emph{American Political Science Review}, \emph{American Journal of Political Science}, and \emph{The Journal of Politics} that use TWFE for causal inference in observational panel settings. Their contribution is to reorient the reanalysis agenda. In many applications, heterogeneity-robust estimators remain qualitatively close to TWFE. Sign reversals and precision losses occur, but they are not the modal outcome. In many cases, the sharper problem lies with identification rather than with weighting. Some studies produce direct evidence against parallel trends. Many others are too underpowered to rule out violations large enough to change the practical meaning of the estimate. This dissertation follows that reorientation from robustness-to-heterogeneity toward parallel-trends credibility, applies it to a different field, and adds an explicit ranking of design margins.

\textcite{brodeur2026reproducibility} contribute a useful distinction. They separate computational reproducibility from robustness. A paper may be computationally reproducible if its code recovers the reported numbers, yet still fragile to alternative but reasonable analytical choices. The exercise in this dissertation tests robustness under re-estimation with estimators developed after publication rather than whether each article's original code reproduces the published estimates.

\\subsubsection{The gap this dissertation fills}
\label{sec:ch2_gap}

These studies leave a gap. \textcite{baker2022much} study corporate finance and accounting. \textcite{chiu2026causal} study political science. \textcite{brodeur2026reproducibility} study robustness across fields. None examines health economics at scale. None documents, within a single applied subfield, which design margins account for the divergence between TWFE and modern estimators. In this reanalysis, the main margins are binning, geographic conditioning, dynamic aggregation, covariate handling, and software defaults.

Health economics suits this exercise for three reasons. First, DiD is a workhorse design in the field, and its use has expanded over the past decade \parencite{goldsmith2024tracking}. Second, the policy stakes are explicit. Many of the papers we re-estimate inform welfare or cost-effectiveness calculations in which effect magnitude, not only sign, matters for the policy conclusion \parencite{stinnett1998net}. Third, health economics has developed its own applied guidance for DiD design and implementation \parencite{wing2018designing}, which provides a benchmark for interpreting the reanalysis results.

This dissertation trades breadth across fields for depth across design margins within one field. It documents how estimator swaps alter applied conclusions, which margin drives the change, and whether the difference reflects design correction, estimand redefinition, or implementation artifact. Chapter~\ref{Chapter4} organizes the results around those margins. The protocol is designed so that the same exercise can be carried out in any applied subfield where DiD is the workhorse design. Chapter~\ref{Chapter5} returns to that possibility.

\\section{Empirical Design}
\label{Chapter3}

This dissertation reanalyzes 53 replication packages with heterogeneous data structures and software environments, applies a common estimator menu, and produces estimates that are comparable across designs. This chapter describes the sample, the scope of the meta-statistic under two named assumptions, the estimator menu, and the AI-assisted protocol that reconciles the three.

The final sample contains 53 articles. Table~\ref{tab:sample_pipeline} summarizes the pipeline, and Appendix~\ref{AppendixD} documents each stage.

The unit of analysis is the article's main specification. For each study we preserve the central empirical result and re-estimate it with alternative econometric tools. A fixed selection hierarchy limits case-by-case discretion. We prioritize the specification the authors call main, preferred, or baseline. When more than one candidate remains, we select the one estimated on the largest number of observations. Three tie-breakers follow in sequence. We prefer the most aggregated outcome, then the specification with fewer time-varying covariates, and as a last resort a documented random draw. When an article reports only a dynamic event study with no comparable static ATT, we re-estimate a collapsed version with a single post-treatment indicator while preserving the original sample, controls, fixed effects, and clustering level. All decisions are recorded in the auditable log described in Appendix~\ref{AppendixA}. Table~\ref{tab:sample_detailed} reports the joint distribution of adoption type and data structure, which jointly determine estimator coverage and counterfactual construction, along with the journal distribution. The sample spans vaccination, insurance regulation, opioids, pollution, maternal and child health, tax credits, and epidemics. Appendix~\ref{AppendixB} lists the reanalyzed studies.

\\subsection{Identification and scope of the meta-statistic}
\label{sec:identification}

The central meta-statistic of this dissertation is the share of the 53 reanalyzed articles whose original TWFE sign is preserved under the standardized modern-estimator protocol.\footnote{At the narrowest level, this quantity describes the sign-preservation rate within the subset of published health-economics studies for which replication materials could be located and the headline specification could be re-estimated under a common protocol.} Reading it as a population parameter for a representative published health-economics DiD study requires two maintained assumptions. We state them now and use the same names throughout.

\textit{Assumption 1 (Sample Representativeness).} The 53-paper sample is informative about the larger population of articles flagged as likely DiD at Step~2 of the sample-construction pipeline (Table~\ref{tab:sample_pipeline}) along the design margins that drive TWFE-versus-modern divergence. These margins include control-group choice, covariate set, dynamic aggregation, endpoint binning, geographic conditioning, and software defaults. The assumption is only partly testable. Balance on these design margins is unobservable for the full Step~2 pool. Table~\ref{tab:selection_balance} compares the reanalyzed sample with a comparison pool of 484 non-reanalyzed articles on observable publication characteristics. Reanalyzed articles are more recent on average and concentrate in AEJ-family journals and \emph{Review of Economics and Statistics}, while field journals are under-represented. The pattern is consistent with selection through replication-material availability rather than random sampling from the published DiD literature. The 53 papers should be read as the replication-package-available subset of the health-economics DiD literature, skewed toward journals with stricter reproducibility policies. Attrition from 1,884 Step~2 articles to 53 is large and concentrated in the stage that combines manual validation with replication-material search, so the headline preservation rate is plausibly biased toward the more careful end of the published distribution.

\input{Tables/table_3_selection_balance.tex}

\textit{Assumption 2 (Estimator Recovery).} Conditional on each article's declared specification and the design margins above, the re-estimation protocol recovers the prespecified modern counterpart defined by this dissertation's standardized decision rules. The assumption concerns execution fidelity. It does not require knowing which estimator the original authors would have chosen had they written the paper using the modern DiD toolkit. The protocol is deterministic for a fixed random seed, and the ten held-out calibration articles (Appendix~\ref{AppendixC}) match the hand-coded benchmark.

If either assumption fails in a direction correlated with the survival outcome, the headline meta-statistic is a descriptive fact about the 53-article sample, not a population parameter. We frame the findings as diagnoses of the studies we re-estimated and flag any extrapolation explicitly.

Because Sample Representativeness is untested, we complement the within-sample preservation rate with worst-case bounds in the style of \textcite{manski1989anatomy}. Of the 53 reanalyzed articles, 46 preserve the sign of the original TWFE estimate under the modern-estimator protocol, so the within-sample preservation rate is $\hat\pi_1 = 46/53 = 86.8\%$. Without further assumptions, the implied preservation rate across the full Step~2 candidate pool lies in the uninformative Manski interval $[2.4\%, 99.6\%]$. Under Manski--Pepper monotone selection, inclusion in the reanalyzed sample is weakly positively associated with sign preservation under modern re-estimation, which gives $\pi \leq 86.8\%$. The within-sample rate is best read as an upper bound on the population parameter, not an unbiased estimate. Appendix~\ref{AppendixD} reports the full selection-bound scenarios and the attrition structure behind them.

The next question is what we re-estimate and which estimators are comparable across the designs in the sample.


\\subsection{Estimators and coverage}

We re-estimate each article's main specification with a fixed menu of econometric tools. The two-way fixed effects (TWFE) model is the baseline estimator, and we keep the structure of the original article whenever possible. We do not audit the authors' code line by line. The goal is to reconstruct the original result in a standardized environment and compare it with modern estimators.

Our primary modern benchmark is the estimator of \textcite{callaway2021difference}. It accommodates treatment-effect heterogeneity, makes the comparison group explicit, handles covariates under a conditional-parallel-trends framework, and delivers static and dynamic estimands that map directly to the reporting conventions of the applied literature. We estimate both a never-treated and a not-yet-treated version whenever the design allows. When the original TWFE specification can be matched in CS-DID, we use that matched specification. When the original covariate structure or fixed-effects design cannot be carried over cleanly to the CS-DID template, the comparison falls back to the closest feasible CS-DID counterpart, which in some articles is the version without controls.\footnote{Table~\ref{tab:covariate_coverage} reports how often the original TWFE specification can be matched in CS-DID. We retain the fallback specification in the aggregate comparisons to preserve article coverage under a uniform rule. Some TWFE-versus-CS-DID contrasts then combine the estimator switch with a covariate margin. Lesson~3 examines the conditional-versus-unconditional CS-DID contrast directly.} Sun--Abraham and Gardner (\texttt{did2s}) appear as complementary dynamic cross-checks, not coequal benchmarks. Comparability across estimators is not uniform across articles, especially when the original TWFE specification carries covariate or fixed-effects structures that cannot transfer one-for-one. We discuss the margin explicitly in the results when it changes the conclusion. Appendix~\ref{AppendixB} reports article-level results, event studies, and sensitivity analyses. Implementing this estimator menu consistently across heterogeneous replication packages requires a separate protocol, described next.

\\subsection{AI-assisted estimation and calibration protocol}

Re-estimating 53 articles by hand does not scale and does not produce outputs comparable across studies. The protocol separates manual specification recovery from standardized estimation, with a continuous audit layer on top. The infrastructure is a reusable component of the dissertation's replication protocol. It follows the separation-of-reasoning-and-execution logic in \textcite{xu2026scaling} and fits within the use of generative AI in economic research surveyed by \textcite{korinek2023generative}. The workflow has three stages. First, the article PDF and replication files feed the recovery of a structured specification file. Second, that file feeds a fixed estimation template that runs all applicable estimators and produces comparable static and dynamic outputs. Third, the results assemble into a standardized comparison with the published finding. The estimation template does not vary across articles. Differences enter through the recovered specification and through documented preprocessing choices. Figure~\ref{fig:ai_workflow} summarizes the workflow, and Appendix~\ref{AppendixC} describes the implementation and metadata structure.\footnote{The structured specification files, estimation scripts, outputs, and protocol-revision logs are available in the replication package.}


We developed the protocol through supervised calibration on 10 articles previously analyzed by hand in Stata. For each article, the workflow reconstructed the design from scratch in R under the standardized execution rules, and the resulting estimates were compared with the manual benchmark. The cross-software separation avoids calibrating within the same implementation stack as the hand-coded analyses and makes software-specific differences easier to detect. Discrepancies triggered diagnostic review and protocol revision until the automated reconstruction matched the benchmark closely enough for replication use. The process produced a catalog of recurring implementation problems and corresponding resolution rules, reported in Appendix~\ref{AppendixC}.\footnote{Examples include Stata date encodings, missing standard errors in plotting routines, silent dropping of event-time horizons, singular matrices in small-cohort CS-DID estimation, and differences in event-study aggregation across software.} The calibrated protocol was then applied to the remaining 43 articles in R. Human review remained active throughout. The researcher reviewed each recovered specification, inspected each event-study output, and approved decisions involving sample restrictions, treatment timing, controls, and cross-estimator comparability. Additional preprocessing, when needed, was encoded through standardized specification fields and documented for replication.\footnote{These cases included composite treatment indicators, time-varying treatment within units, and individual-level repeated cross-sections requiring aggregation.}

A per-article audit layer verifies implementation fidelity. The layer checks each estimator against package documentation, compares stored coefficients with the values reported in the original paper, and writes article-level audit reports included in the replication package. Aggregate fidelity statistics from this audit feed the eligibility gate at Step~4 of the sample-construction pipeline (Table~\ref{tab:sample_pipeline}).

The audit layer is what distinguishes this protocol from a single-paper replication exercise. For each article, the methodology of every estimator (CS-DID, TWFE, Bacon, HonestDiD, and de Chaisemartin) is evaluated against the official package documentation and the recent literature, the stored estimates are compared against the paper's published values, and the verdicts are synthesized into a per-article credibility rating on a three-axis rubric. The 24-check aggregate audit and the per-article reports are included in the replication package and are reusable for reanalysis exercises in other fields. Appendix~\ref{AppendixC} documents the audit protocol, the rubric, and the implementation details.

\\section{Lessons from the reanalysis}
\label{Chapter4}

Health-policy recommendations depend on a DiD estimate's magnitude relative to a decision threshold, not on its sign alone. When a modern estimator shifts the estimate by more than the policy-relevant distance, the recommendation can change. This chapter traces where in the sample those shifts concentrate. Lesson 1 establishes that the literature is directionally stable and quantitatively fragile. Lesson 2 locates that fragility in the aggregation structure and control-group choice of staggered designs. Lesson 3 traces it to covariate and geographic conditioning. Lesson 4 examines the dynamic profile, where persistence claims are sensitive to the endpoint. Lesson 5 separates robustness to treatment-effect heterogeneity from robustness to parallel-trends violations. Lesson 6 shows that aggregation rules and software balancing can change the estimand and the effective sample. Lesson 7 documents a complementary finding. Modern estimators clarify the question without sharpening the answer, because their confidence intervals are often wider than the TWFE benchmark. Lesson 8 closes the empirical arc by asking when the magnitude instability documented in Lessons 1--6 translates into welfare instability, using the Marginal Value of Public Funds framework of \textcite{hendren2020unified}. The scope is narrow. The sample contains 53 health-economics studies with usable replication packages, and the reanalysis depends on the data and code environments the original authors provided. The chapter closes with two artefacts distilled from these margins, a pre-submission checklist and a design-triage guide for modern DiD.

\\subsection{Lesson 1. The literature is directionally stable, but quantitatively fragile}

A common reading of sign preservation is that the TWFE result has survived. That reading is incomplete. Replacing TWFE with modern DiD estimators preserves the direction of most published findings and changes their quantitative content. Across the 53-article sample, the sign of the estimated effect is preserved in 46 cases and reverses in 7. The larger differences appear in magnitude and precision. Losses of statistical significance are more common than gains, and large re-scaling of the estimate is frequent. Table~\ref{tab:ch3_aggregate_concordance} reports the full classification.

\input{Tables/table_4_1_aggregate_concordance.tex}

Figures~\ref{fig:ch3_aggregate_variation} and \ref{fig:ch3_aggregate_scatter} extend the same point.\footnote{Both figures use the closest feasible CS-DID counterpart to each article's TWFE specification. Some comparisons also reflect the covariate margin. Table~\ref{tab:covariate_coverage} reports the coverage counts, and Lesson~3 examines that margin directly.} In Figure~\ref{fig:ch3_aggregate_variation}, most articles remain on the same side of zero, but the dispersion in proportional changes is wide and the tails are large. As Table~\ref{tab:ch3_aggregate_concordance} shows, 21 articles (39.6\%) change by more than 50\% in magnitude and 13 (24.5\%) change by more than 100\%. Figure~\ref{fig:ch3_aggregate_scatter} makes the same point in standardized units. Many estimates lie near the 45-degree line, but the cloud is loose. A sizeable share moves enough to change statistical precision or to alter the implied effect size.

Policy evaluation depends on effect size relative to a decision threshold, not on sign alone \parencite{johannesson1993decision,stinnett1998net,claxton1999irrelevance,hendren2020unified,finkelstein2020welfare}. Two estimates can share a sign and still support different decisions when the relevant threshold lies between them. Modern estimators leave much of the literature directionally intact while making its policy content less stable. Lesson 2 shows that this magnitude instability clusters in staggered designs. Subsection~\ref{sec:mvpf_stress_test} returns to the welfare consequences.

\\subsection{Lesson 2. In staggered designs, aggregation structure and control-group choice do the heavy lifting}

The modern critique of TWFE is often read as a critique about negative weights. In this sample, negative weights are not the dominant margin. The sharpest disagreements between TWFE and modern DiD estimators arise in staggered-adoption designs, and the mechanism is aggregation structure and control-group choice. Table~\ref{tab:ch3_timing_heterogeneity} shows that sign reversals appear only in the staggered subsample and that large changes in magnitude are far more common there than in one-time-adoption designs.\footnote{In the one-time-adoption subsample, sign reversals are absent. In the staggered subsample, they appear in 24.1\% of articles. The median absolute change in magnitude rises from 17.6\% to 64.7\%, and the shares with \(|\Delta|>50\%\) and \(|\Delta|>100\%\) rise from 16.7\% to 58.6\% and from 8.3\% to 37.9\%, respectively.} One-time-adoption designs are not fully stable, but the largest disagreements in the sample are concentrated in staggered adoption.
\input{Tables/table_4_2_timing_heterogeneity.tex}

Negative weights explain only part of that pattern. Table~\ref{tab:bacon_summary} shows that they are common but modest in size.\footnote{In the dCdH decomposition, negative weights appear in 21 of 27 staggered articles. Their mean share is 6.4\%, the median is 2.5\%, and the maximum is 23.8\%.} The larger issue is the comparison structure induced by staggered timing. In the Bacon decomposition, treated-versus-treated comparisons account for a large share of the TWFE coefficient on average and often dominate it.\footnote{The treated-versus-treated share is 34.9\% on average and exceeds 50\% in 8 of 26 articles.} Once much of the coefficient is built from comparisons between already-treated and later-treated units, the estimate no longer maps cleanly to a single causal contrast.

\\subsubsection{Aggregation and the Maclean \& Pabilonia (2025) case}

\textcite{maclean2025paid} makes this problem concrete. In our replication, TWFE is positive while CS-DID with never-treated controls is negative.\footnote{The TWFE estimate is \(+4.61\) minutes per day (SE~1.80, \(p<0.05\)), and our reanalysis reproduces it exactly. The corresponding CS-DID estimate with never-treated controls is \(-15.27\) (SE~6.26, \(p<0.05\)).}

Figure~\ref{fig:bacon_dcdh_maclean} points to the mechanism. One cohort receives large weight under size-weighted estimators, and that cohort activates in 2020, when parental childcare time was sharply affected by the COVID-19 lockdowns. TWFE and other size-weighted estimators place that influence on the cohort most exposed to the shock. CS-DID distributes weight more evenly across group-time cells and recovers the broader negative pattern.\footnote{The design has six adoption cohorts. Because treatment is lagged two years, the 2018-adopter cohort activates in 2020. Its treated-versus-untreated \(2\times2\) ATT is \(+10.1\) and it absorbs 34\% of TWFE weight. The 2017 cohort carries the largest weight share (48\%) but a mildly negative ATT (\(-4.0\)). The 2022 cohort has a strongly negative ATT (\(-21.5\)) at 10\% weight. Each cohort's contribution to the pseudo-panel TWFE (weight \(\times\) ATT) is \(0.48\times(-4.0)=-1.92\) for 2017, \(0.34\times(+10.12)=+3.44\) for 2020, and \(0.10\times(-21.5)=-2.15\) for 2022, summing to approximately \(-0.4\). The 2020 cohort is the only positive contributor of any size, and without it the TWFE would align with CS-DID's negative estimate. Without covariates, TWFE falls to \(+1.82\) (n.s.) while CS-DID remains at \(-15.27\), so most of the gap survives. The dCdH decomposition returns zero negative weights across \(484\) \((g,t)\) cells, and the Bacon decomposition assigns 7.9\% of TWFE weight to treated-versus-treated comparisons. Appendix Table~10 of \textcite{maclean2025paid} also reports positive or null estimates for Gardner, \textcite{borusyak2024revisiting}, \textcite{wooldridge2025two}, and Stacked DiD.}

The sign reversal reflects a difference in aggregation rules in a staggered design where one heavily weighted cohort is unusually exposed to a confounding shock.

\\subsubsection{Control-group choice as a second staggered margin}

A common practice in CS-DID is to run one control-group choice, usually never-treated, and treat the other as a robustness check. In staggered designs that ordering is backward. The control group is part of identification and should be defended first. It determines the untreated path used to identify the effect and, with it, the relevant parallel-trends assumption. Choosing between never-treated (NT) and not-yet-treated (NYT) is part of the design. As \textcite{marcus2021role} emphasize, staggered-DiD procedures can rely on different parallel-trends assumptions even when they target the same group-time effects. NT- and NYT-based designs need not encode the same counterfactual path. The applied question is which untreated path is more credible in the setting.

NT and NYT do not simply reweight the same design. They can rely on different comparison sets as treatment spreads over time, and they can imply different restrictions on untreated outcome dynamics. When the two approaches produce similar estimates, the result does not depend heavily on the counterfactual strategy. That does not make the choice irrelevant. When they diverge, the difference is informative. The design is being identified from different untreated paths, not just estimated with different software defaults.

Figure~\ref{fig:nt_nyt_examples} illustrates both margins. In some applications, NT and NYT rely on nearly the same comparison units and produce almost identical dynamic profiles. In others, the never-treated group is thin and the NYT pool shrinks as adoption spreads. The estimators are then anchored to different counterfactuals, and divergence emerges as a design result rather than a numerical detail. In staggered designs, the control-group choice should be justified as an identifying assumption, not treated as a minor robustness check.

\\subsection{Lesson 3. Covariates and geographic conditioning change the identifying comparison}

A common practice is to add covariates to a TWFE regression and read the coefficient as conditional on observables. In a CS-DID comparison, that step changes the identifying contrast, not only the precision. Part of the TWFE--CS-DID gap in this sample runs through covariate handling. In many published applications, the headline TWFE specification includes covariates or additional fixed effects, while the feasible CS-DID replication is unconditional. That comparison does not only swap estimators. It also changes the identifying comparison. Table~\ref{tab:covariate_coverage} reports how often the original covariate structure can be carried over to CS-DID and how often the comparison falls back to an unconditional specification.

This is an identification issue, not a coding detail. When covariates are invoked to support parallel trends, the relevant claim is conditional parallel trends. When they are included only for precision, their role differs. Baseline covariates can strengthen the design when untreated trends differ systematically across observable groups. Time-varying covariates are more delicate because they may respond to treatment or lie on the causal path \parencite{caetano2022difference,caetano_callaway2024_hiddenlinearity}.

To isolate this margin, we estimate a matched-protocol CS-DID that uses the paper's covariate set whenever the specification is feasible. Figure~\ref{fig:density_covariates} compares TWFE against CS-DID with and without controls on the common subset of articles for which both specifications are available and non-degenerate. Aligning covariates does not systematically move CS-DID closer to TWFE. In some articles, the gap narrows. In others, it widens. The TWFE--CS-DID gap is not an estimator-swap artifact alone. Part of it reflects a change in the estimand induced by conditioning choices.

That pattern matches the recent methodological literature. Under TWFE with covariates, the fixed-effects transformation changes how the regression conditions on \(X\), and the resulting estimand need not coincide with the \(ATT(g,t)\) recovered by CS-DID under conditional parallel trends \parencite{callaway2021difference,caetano2022difference,caetano_callaway2024_hiddenlinearity,wooldridge2025two}. A matched-protocol comparison sharpens the empirical contrast, but it does not eliminate that underlying estimand difference.

\\subsubsection{Geographic conditioning as a special case}

A common refinement is to condition on region or state to strengthen design credibility. Finer geographic conditioning can trade identification against support rather than strengthen one without cost. In TWFE, time-invariant geographic indicators are absorbed by unit fixed effects, so geographic adjustment appears through interacted trends such as region-by-time or state-by-time effects. In CS-DID, geography can instead enter directly in the nuisance models. That makes geographic conditioning explicit, and it makes overlap a first-order concern.

Figure~\ref{fig:graduated_sensitivity} traces this trade-off. The exercise starts from a baseline specification, adds progressively finer geographic controls, and re-estimates the state-conditioned model after restricting the sample to states with minimum treated and control support. The four panels illustrate the main empirical possibilities. In some articles, finer geographic conditioning attenuates the estimate and that attenuation survives the overlap restriction, consistent with geography absorbing relevant confounding variation. In others, the estimate collapses under full state conditioning and reappears once weak-support states are removed, more suggestive of support-driven instability than of a design correction. A third set remains broadly stable across specifications, indicating that geographic conditioning is not first-order. A fourth set is harder to interpret because the overlap-restricted estimate shifts sharply, so changes in support and changes in the target population are intertwined.

Geographic conditioning is useful when the design raises a clear geographic confounding concern and when overlap remains credible after conditioning. Finer geographic controls are not automatically an improvement. In CS-DID, they can trade one problem for another by stabilizing identification along one margin while weakening support along another. Geographic conditioning should be treated as a sensitivity margin, not as a default refinement of the baseline specification. With the static margins clarified, the dynamic profile raises its own questions. Lesson 4 addresses them.

\\subsection{Lesson 4. Dynamic reanalysis changes the story about timing and persistence}

A common practice is to collapse the event study into a single post-treatment dummy. Doing so hides three distinct patterns. Dynamic reanalysis often leaves the short-run direction of the result intact while changing the inferred persistence of the effect, the importance of control-group choice, and the reading of pre-treatment dynamics. Figure~\ref{fig:ch3_eventstudy_panel} collects six cases that illustrate these margins.

Some applications remain stable. In \textcite{dranove2021dose}, TWFE, CS-NT, and CS-NYT trace nearly the same post-treatment decline, and the main difference is wider uncertainty at longer horizons. Other cases are sensitive to the comparison group. In \textcite{carpenter2019direct}, TWFE tracks CS-NT closely, while CS-NYT is systematically smaller after treatment, pointing to a weaker effect under the broader comparison. A third set of cases disagrees about persistence rather than the initial post-treatment response. In \textcite{lawler2023effect}, all estimators show a positive short-run effect, but the CS profiles weaken at the end of the event window while TWFE remains persistently positive. In \textcite{bosch2014tradeoffs}, TWFE becomes increasingly negative over time, whereas the CS estimates show a smaller decline that fades at longer horizons. \textcite{cao2023mitigating} runs in the opposite direction. TWFE turns negative after treatment, and both CS estimators become more negative at longer horizons, implying a larger long-run effect under the robust estimators. Finally, \textcite{brodeur2025shootings} is diagnostic. All three specifications show sizeable leads before treatment together with a negative post-treatment path, so the concern about design credibility is shared across estimators rather than revealed by the re-estimation.

Event studies should be read as design diagnostics, not as automatic summaries of causal dynamics. Reanalysis can leave the headline sign unchanged and still change what the paper says about when effects begin, how long they last, and whether the identifying assumptions remain credible. That interpretation depends on the control group, the support available at longer horizons, and on whether apparent pre-trend problems are estimator-specific or common across specifications. Dynamic profiles diverge most visibly at long horizons, where support thins and endpoint binning compounds the problem.

\\subsubsection{Long tails and endpoint binning}
\label{sec:binning}

Long-horizon coefficients are the least credible part of a dynamic DiD design. In 16 of 21 papers with dynamic event studies in this sample, the far-post coefficient moves by more than 20\% under progressive-binning exercises. Two distinct problems become severe at long horizons. The first is a data problem. Support thins as event time lengthens, and the composition of contributing cohorts changes with the horizon. This affects all event-study estimators. Changing estimators does not recover horizons that the data do not support. The second problem is specific to TWFE. Each event-study coefficient is a weighted average of CATTs from multiple relative periods, not just the target horizon, so heterogeneous effects at distant horizons enter coefficients that readers interpret as local dynamic effects. Modern estimators remove this cross-horizon contamination by building event-study paths from clean group-time averages. They do not remove the support problem.

Endpoint binning makes this margin harder to read. Pooling all periods below $-K_{\text{pre}}$ and above $K_{\text{post}}$ into far-pre and far-post indicators keeps the specification estimable, and it also changes the estimand. The far-post coefficient no longer corresponds to a single horizon. It becomes a summary shaped by cross-period contamination, implicit within-bin weighting, and compositional change across horizons.\footnote{For contamination in TWFE event studies, see \textcite{sun2021estimating}. For weighting distortions induced by binning, see \textcite{borusyak2023did_imputation}. For horizon-specific compositional change in staggered designs, see \textcite{callaway2021difference}. For compositional changes in repeated cross-sections, see \textcite{sant2026difference}.}

\\paragraph*{The Lawler \& Yewell (2023) case}

\textcite{lawler2023effect} study the effect of Baby-Friendly Hospital designation on breastfeeding initiation using a repeated cross-section of U.S.\ birth records, with county and birth-year fixed effects, an event window of $[-4,+4]$, and endpoint bins $F_5$ ($e \leq -5$) and $L_4$ ($e \geq 4$). This is a common applied specification, and it is the kind of design in which the tail can look more informative than it is. The post-treatment pattern is positive under both TWFE and CS-NYT, and the far-post coefficient is not stable. The binned TWFE specification delivers a far-post estimate of about $+0.047$. Replacing the $L_4$ bin with one dummy per horizon, estimated on the full sample, lowers the coefficient at $t=+4$ to about $+0.032$, and the CS-NYT estimate at the same horizon is smaller still.

The gap is informative because the binned $L_4$ coefficient is not a clean estimate of the effect at $e=4$. The \textcite{sun2022eventstudyinteract} decomposition shows that the far-post coefficient loads on remote post-treatment horizons and places non-zero weight on pre-treatment periods. The progressive-binning diagnostic in Table~\ref{tab:progbin_sensitivity} shows the same point in coefficient space. Holding the $L_4$ position fixed and varying only how many post-treatment horizons it absorbs, the coefficient rises from $+0.031$ when the bin contains horizon $4$ alone ($k = 0$) to $+0.047$ when it absorbs horizons $4$ through $16$ ($k_{\max} = 12$, $t = 4$). The fully unbinned estimate at $t=+4$ is $+0.032$, where the progressive-binning sequence begins. The increase up to $+0.047$ is not evidence that persistence at $e=4$ is stronger than the unbinned estimate suggests. As the endpoint bin absorbs additional post-treatment horizons, the coefficient becomes a summary of a larger treated tail. The published far-post estimate is weak evidence on persistence at a specific horizon. Claims about long-run persistence based on that endpoint should be read cautiously, because part of the estimated persistence comes from the binning rule rather than from clean horizon-specific evidence.

\\subsection{Lesson 5. Robustness to heterogeneity does not imply robustness to parallel-trends violations}

Switching from TWFE to a heterogeneity-robust estimator can improve the mapping between the estimate and the causal parameter. It does not, by itself, make the identifying counterfactual more credible. In this sample, that distinction is consequential. Table~\ref{tab:ch3_honestdid} shows that sensitivity to departures from exact parallel trends remains widespread under both TWFE and CS-DID.

The pattern is similar across estimators. In both the first post-treatment period and the peak post-treatment coefficient, the median breakdown value is zero, and the share of articles with $\bar M = 0$ is high. Strong robustness is uncommon in either column. Cleaning the estimand does not, in most cases, remove fragility in the counterfactual path.\footnote{The breakdown value $\bar{M}_{\text{peak}}$ reported in Table~\ref{tab:ch3_honestdid} targets the peak event-study coefficient selected by the largest absolute $t$-statistic, not the aggregate ATT. When the conventional confidence interval of the aggregate already covers zero, the aggregate breakdown is trivially zero. The peak-coefficient breakdown can still be positive if individual event-time coefficients remain statistically significant. Readers comparing the per-article cards (Appendix~\ref{AppendixB}) should read $\bar{M}_{\text{peak}}$ as describing the peak dynamic coefficient, whereas concordance between TWFE and CS-DID in Chapter~\ref{Chapter4}, Lesson~1, refers to the static aggregate.}

This is the design-based margin emphasized by \textcite{baker2025difference}. Estimator choice and counterfactual credibility are related, and they are not the same question. In the reanalyzed sample, sensitivity to parallel-trends violations survives the move from TWFE to CS-DID.

That comparison requires one qualification. HonestDiD, as developed by \textcite{rambachan2023more}, assumes that the input event-study coefficients are unbiased estimates of the causal effect at each horizon. In staggered-adoption designs, TWFE event-study coefficients do not satisfy that condition. \textcite{sun2021estimating} show that each TWFE coefficient is a weighted average of cohort-specific treatment effects from multiple relative periods, not only the target horizon. The pre-treatment coefficients that HonestDiD uses as its anchor can reflect heterogeneous post-treatment effects. The TWFE column of Table~\ref{tab:ch3_honestdid} should be read as the sensitivity bound an applied researcher obtains by applying HonestDiD directly to the TWFE event study. It is an applied benchmark, not a clean causal benchmark.

The CS-DID column has the standard Rambachan--Roth interpretation. It feeds HonestDiD with group-time ATTs whose pre-treatment coefficients are uncontaminated by construction. That makes it the more interpretable benchmark for design fragility once TWFE's weighting problem is set aside. The main result does not depend on that distinction. Even under this cleaner input, the breakdown distributions are qualitatively similar. Median $\bar M$ is zero at both the first and peak horizons, and the share with $\bar M = 0$ ranges from 52\% to 75\% across cells. The fragility diagnosed by HonestDiD remains visible after the move from TWFE to CS-DID. Once estimand and identifying assumption are both defended, the remaining question is whether software implementation can shift the result. Lesson 6 takes up the aggregation rule and the effective sample.

\\subsection{Lesson 6. Implementation choices shape the estimand and the effective sample}

A common reading is that group, simple, and dynamic aggregation are display conventions. They are part of the estimand. In CS-DID, the comparison with TWFE depends on the underlying \(ATT(g,t)\) structure and on how those effects are aggregated.\footnote{The three main aggregation schemes are group, simple, and dynamic. The first averages within cohorts and then across cohorts. The second averages directly across all group-time cells. The third averages across cohorts at each event-time horizon and then collapses the post-treatment profile into a single effect.} The choice matters empirically. Table~\ref{tab:ch3_aggte_comparison} shows the classification of the TWFE vs.\ CS-DID comparison under each aggregation rule. The group aggregation is available for all 53 articles. The dynamic and simple aggregations are available for 52 each, because one article lacks a dynamic profile. Dynamic aggregation yields the highest concordance with TWFE, while group and simple aggregation generate more reversals and more cases of divergence.

Changing the aggregation rule changes the empirical object being compared with TWFE. In some applications, the difference is small. In others, it is large enough to alter the classification of the result. Aggregation is part of the estimand. A CS-DID estimate is not fully defined unless the aggregation rule is stated, and any comparison with TWFE is only interpretable once that choice is explicit. Beyond aggregation, a second estimand choice shapes the comparison even when the estimator label stays fixed. That choice is how the applied researcher handles an unbalanced panel. \textcite{callaway2021difference}, Remark~8 in Section~3.1.1, formalise it as a robustness-versus-efficiency trade-off that lives inside the event-study parameter.

\\subsubsection{Unbalanced panels as an estimand choice}
\label{sec:li_sensitivity}

The handling of an unbalanced panel is a choice of estimand, not a coding detail. \textcite{callaway2021difference} make this explicit. Section~3.1.1 of their paper distinguishes two event-study parameters. The unbalanced parameter $\theta_{es}(e)$ averages $ATT(g, g+e)$ across whatever cohorts are observed at event time $e$, so the set of groups contributing to $\theta_{es}(e)$ shifts with $e$. The balanced parameter $\theta_{es}^{\mathrm{bal}}(e; e')$ restricts the average to cohorts observed for at least $e'$ post-treatment periods, holding cohort composition fixed across the event-time profile. The two parameters estimate different causal objects. Changes in $\theta_{es}^{\mathrm{bal}}$ across $e$ reflect within-cohort dynamics. Changes in $\theta_{es}(e)$ across $e$ conflate within-cohort dynamics with shifts in which cohorts enter the average at each horizon.\footnote{\textcite{callaway2021difference}, Remark~8, frame this explicitly as a ``robustness versus efficiency trade-off'': balancing protects against compositional artefacts at the cost of dropping cohorts, so the standard errors of $\theta_{es}^{\mathrm{bal}}(e; e')$ are larger than those of $\theta_{es}(e)$.}

Empirical defaults across software reflect this trade-off rather than introduce it. The R \texttt{did} package exposes \texttt{allow\_unbalanced\_panel} and \texttt{balance\_e} so the applied researcher can target either parameter. The Stata \texttt{csdid2} default balances each $(g,t)$ comparison pair-by-pair, a third, intermediate point in the same family. When the panel is close to balanced, the three points collapse to approximately the same number. When the panel has substantial attrition, they identify genuinely different parameters, and the magnitude of the gap depends on whether compositional shifts across $e$ covary with the treatment effect.

Figure~\ref{fig:sensitivity_210} makes that gap visible in \textcite{li2025shortened}. Under the fully balanced R construction ($\theta_{es}^{\mathrm{bal}}$) and the pair-balanced Stata default, post-treatment effects are positive throughout most of the event window. Under the unbalanced R construction ($\theta_{es}$), the profile turns mostly negative after treatment and also shows a visible pre-treatment dip. The identifying design is unchanged. What changes is which CS parameter is being reported. The unbalanced profile absorbs a composition shift, with later-treated cohorts entering the average at longer horizons, that the balanced profile holds fixed by construction.

The CS-DID estimate is not fully defined until the applied researcher states which event-study parameter she is targeting and why. When fill rates are near unity, the choice is immaterial and any default is defensible. When fill rates are meaningfully below unity, the researcher should report the parameter, the fill rate, and a sensitivity check across the three natural points in the trade-off (balanced, pair-balanced, unbalanced). Appendix~\ref{AppendixB} reports panel fill rates for each article in the sample, and Appendix~\ref{AppendixC} documents the article-level choices and exceptions.\footnote{One additional mechanical margin concerns the choice of base period. In this reanalysis, all estimates were standardised to a universal base period so that the comparison isolates the unbalanced-panel choice rather than the base-period choice.} The six margins above improve the mapping between the estimate and the causal parameter. They do not always improve precision. Lesson 7 examines the cases where modern estimators clarify the question without sharpening the answer.

\\subsection{Lesson 7. When modern estimators do not help}
\label{sec:when_modern_fails}

A familiar reading of modern DiD is that it replaces TWFE with a cleaner estimate. That reading is too optimistic. Modern estimators clarify the question by making the estimand explicit, and they widen the confidence interval at the same time. In some designs, they do not resolve the disagreement with TWFE. They relocate it, from a question about aggregation bias to a question about precision or propensity-score stability. This lesson isolates two cases where switching estimators changes what the reader knows without changing what the reader can conclude.

\\subsubsection{Precision loss is the rule, not the exception}

CS-DID confidence intervals are wider than TWFE confidence intervals in most of the sample. Across the 53 articles, the median ratio of the CS-NT standard error to the TWFE standard error is 1.31, and 68\% of articles have a ratio above 1. 42\% of articles have a ratio above 1.5, and 20\% have a ratio above 2. The sharpest case in the sample has a ratio of about 15.

This widening has a direct consequence for the significance pattern. Of 41 articles with statistically significant TWFE coefficients, 13 lose significance under CS-NT. Only 2 move in the opposite direction. The cleaner estimand does not vanish. Point estimates remain close to TWFE in most cases. What changes is the confidence interval, which covers zero in roughly a quarter of the cases where TWFE excluded it.

Loss of significance is not evidence against the published result. It is evidence that the cleaner estimand is harder to pin down in the design the paper used. When TWFE averages over many $2\times2$ comparisons at a cost of interpretability, the averaging also buys precision. When CS-DID builds the aggregate from clean group-time cells, those cells are smaller, and the variance of the aggregate reflects that. Readers who treat CI widening as refutation of the TWFE result misread what the reanalysis shows.

\\subsubsection{Doubly-robust instability: clean, collapse, inflate}

A second margin shows up when the paper's covariate structure is carried over to CS-DID as a matched-protocol specification. The \texttt{did} package's doubly-robust estimator fits a propensity-score model for each $(g,t)$ cell. When the covariate set is well-behaved, the matched specification returns an estimate close to the unconditional one. When it is not, the propensity-score model either collapses to the null or produces extreme weights that inflate the estimate. Three articles in the sample illustrate the three canonical behaviours.

\textcite{carrillo2019provider} use 18 pre-treatment variables interacted with trends in a staggered design. The matched-protocol CS-DID estimate tracks the unconditional estimate closely. The matched and unconditional specifications agree.

\textcite{carpenter2019direct} add a high-dimensional set of demographic and state-level controls to a staggered design. The propensity-score model overfits. The matched-protocol ATT collapses to zero, and the estimation either fails to converge or returns a degenerate standard error. The matched specification becomes uninformative.

\textcite{levine2011effective} is a null-result paper on a repeated cross-section with time-varying individual-level demographic controls. The matched-protocol specification converges, and the logistic regression for the propensity score produces extreme weights because the individual covariates have no within-unit continuity. The matched-protocol ATT inflates by a factor of about nine relative to the unconditional estimate, and crosses the significance threshold as a false positive.

The three cases do not show that doubly-robust CS-DID is unreliable. They show that the estimator interacts with the covariate set in ways that the convergence flag does not reveal. Applied researchers cannot choose between the matched and unconditional specifications on convergence alone. The matched estimate must be read against the unconditional benchmark and against the dimension of the covariate vector.

\\subsubsection{Implication}

A disagreement between TWFE and CS-DID is not automatically evidence against TWFE. It can reflect precision loss or propensity-score pathology. Reading the comparison requires identifying which margin is active. That identification belongs in the paper, not in a referee report. The applied guide below formalises the checks that make each margin visible before the reader has to reconstruct them from raw output.

\\subsection{Lesson 8. Magnitude instability matters when fiscal externalities push net cost toward zero}
\label{sec:mvpf_stress_test}

The preceding lessons show that replacing TWFE with modern DiD estimators often preserves the sign of the published estimate while changing its magnitude. That is not yet a welfare statement. Whether a magnitude change matters depends on the mapping from the coefficient to the policy object that readers use to make a decision. This lesson studies that mapping through the Marginal Value of Public Funds framework of \textcite{hendren2020unified}.

The lesson is deliberately narrower after the implementation audit. The first pass treats the re-estimated coefficients mechanically and asks how much the implied MVPF moves along a fixed welfare curve. The second pass filters those movements through the article-level audit. This distinction matters. A large movement in the mechanical table can be a genuine welfare sensitivity, or it can be the welfare image of an estimator that the audit classifies as non-informative. The policy reading should come from the latter exercise, not from the raw span alone.

\\subsubsection{From the coefficient to welfare: the MVPF mapping}

The starting point is the Marginal Value of Public Funds framework developed by \textcite{hendren2020unified} and exposited in \textcite{finkelstein2020welfare}. A causal estimate identifies how a policy changed an outcome. Welfare analysis requires an additional mapping from that outcome change to beneficiary willingness to pay and net government cost. The MVPF summarises this mapping as
\begin{equation*}
\mathrm{MVPF} \;=\; \frac{\mathrm{WTP}}{G},
\end{equation*}
where \(\mathrm{WTP}\) is beneficiary willingness to pay per dollar of program spending and \(G\) is the net government cost per dollar of program spending.

The denominator can be written as \(G = 1 + \mathrm{FE}\), where \(\mathrm{FE}\) collects fiscal externalities generated by behavioural responses to the policy. These responses can change government spending or revenue outside the program. A health program can change future Medicare and Medicaid spending. An education program that raises future earnings can raise future tax receipts. These downstream budget effects enter \(G\) as part of the welfare-relevant cost of the policy.

The same change in \(\hat\beta\) can have different welfare implications across policies. If fiscal externalities are small, \(G\) remains close to one and the MVPF changes slowly with the estimated effect. If fiscal externalities are large enough to push \(G\) toward zero, the same movement in \(\hat\beta\) produces a large movement in the MVPF. In the limiting case, positive willingness to pay combined with zero or negative net government cost yields an infinite MVPF, and the policy is said to pay for itself.

The coefficient \(\hat\beta\) enters the MVPF through one or both components. In programs whose outcome generates downstream fiscal savings or costs, \(\hat\beta\) feeds \(G\) directly. In programs where the outcome has an explicit beneficiary value, \(\hat\beta\) can also feed \(\mathrm{WTP}\). This lesson asks whether the local mapping from \(\hat\beta\) to \(\mathrm{MVPF}\) is flat or steep. When the mapping is flat and \(G\) is far from zero, large proportional changes in \(\hat\beta\) leave the policy recommendation nearly unchanged. When \(G\) is near zero, the mapping becomes steep and sign preservation no longer guarantees welfare stability.



\\subsubsection{What the stress test does and what it deliberately leaves out}

This lesson does not replicate the full \textcite{hendren2020unified} framework for either paper. A full MVPF exercise would require a paper-specific model of willingness to pay, intertemporal fiscal flows, discounting, and valuation parameters such as the Value of Statistical Life or Statistical Life-Year. The exercise here is a stress test. It holds a simple welfare mapping fixed and asks how much the implied MVPF moves when \(\hat\beta\) moves across alternative estimates.

The simplifications are deliberate. Each paper is mapped to one fiscal-externality channel and one calibration constant. This abstracts from the full set of budgetary channels that a complete welfare analysis would need to model. Community Health Centers can affect Medicare, Medicaid, disability insurance, and other public spending. Managed care can affect drug spending and interact with other parts of Medicaid reimbursement. The exercise collapses those channels because the object of interest is the local slope of \(\mathrm{MVPF}(\hat\beta)\), not the level of the MVPF.

The test is geometric rather than comprehensive. For each paper, I trace the curve implied by the chosen welfare mapping, mark the mechanically generated estimates, and then ask which of those estimates survives the implementation audit. If the coefficients fall on a flat segment, estimator-induced magnitude changes have little welfare consequence. If they fall near the point where \(G\) approaches zero, similar changes in \(\hat\beta\) move the MVPF sharply. The same procedure can be repeated for any paper whose policy channel admits a closed-form mapping from \(\hat\beta\) to \(G\).

Table~\ref{tab:mvpf_stress_test} reports the mechanically matched-control stress test for the two illustrative papers. The table is useful because it shows the geometry of the welfare map. It should not be read as the set of preferred welfare inputs. Subsections~\ref{sec:mvpf_safe} and~\ref{sec:mvpf_laffer} separate the mechanical span from the audit-filtered reading.

\\subsubsection{Safe zone: Dranove, Ody and Starc (2021)}
\label{sec:mvpf_safe}

\textcite{dranove2021dose} estimate the effect of Medicaid managed care on the log price paid per prescription. The relevant fiscal-externality channel is direct. If managed-care contracts reduce drug prices, Medicaid spending on prescription drugs falls. The channel is diluted because the coefficient applies only to the drug component of Medicaid spending. Per-enrollee Medicaid spending in the relevant period allocates roughly \(15\%\) to drugs, leaving the remaining \(85\%\) outside the price index measured by \(\hat\beta\). The calibration is
\begin{equation*}
G(\hat\beta) \;=\; 1 + 0.15\cdot(e^{\hat\beta}-1), \qquad \mathrm{WTP} \;=\; 1.
\end{equation*}

The mechanical matched-control exercise produces a large coefficient movement. TWFE with the paper's control gives \(\hat\beta=-0.176\). The doubly-robust CS estimates that force the same covariate into the propensity-score step are \(-0.013\) in the static aggregation and \(-0.004\) in the dynamic aggregation. Along the welfare curve, this moves \(G\) only from \(0.976\) to \(0.999\) and the MVPF only from \(1.025\) to \(1.001\). The nominal welfare range is \(0.024\) units.

The audit changes the interpretation of that span. The near-zero CS estimates are not the preferred welfare inputs. They are a diagnostic result: in a 29-state balanced panel, forcing the single continuous covariate into the CS propensity-score model produces a panel variant of propensity-score overfit. The estimator converges and reports standard errors, but the resulting CS estimates are non-informative. The audit-filtered comparison instead uses the unconditional matched protocol: TWFE without controls is \(-0.186\), the CS-NT group aggregate is \(-0.208\), the simple aggregate is \(-0.214\), and the dynamic aggregate is \(-0.198\). These values imply MVPFs between \(1.026\) and \(1.030\).

The welfare reading is therefore stable for two reasons. First, the credible estimates are close to each other. Second, even the mechanically collapsed estimates sit on a flat segment of the welfare map. The affected spending category is only \(15\%\) of total Medicaid spending in the calibration, so a large proportional change in \(\hat\beta\) produces a small change in \(G\). Dranove is the safe-zone case: estimator and covariate sensitivity may matter for the econometric diagnosis, but the welfare mapping absorbs it before it reaches the policy recommendation.

\\subsubsection{Laffer zone: Bailey and Goodman-Bacon (2015)}
\label{sec:mvpf_laffer}

\textcite{bailey2015war} estimate the effect of Community Health Center (CHC) rollout on age-adjusted mortality for adults aged \(50\) and over. The fiscal-externality channel differs sharply from the Dranove case. Each elderly death averted by CHC access affects the federal budget through several channels. Longer survival raises future Medicare spending and Social Security payments. At the same time, avoided late-stage hospitalisation, emergency care, and disability-related utilisation generate offsetting savings. The calibration summarises this net channel with
\begin{equation*}
\mathrm{WTP}(\hat\beta) \;=\; 1 + \alpha\cdot(-\hat\beta), \qquad G(\hat\beta) \;=\; 1 + \lambda\cdot\hat\beta,
\end{equation*}
where \(\alpha=\lambda=0.015\) per averted death per \(10^5\) elderly residents.\footnote{The calibration back-solves the mortality valuation and fiscal-savings terms from the Medicare-Introduction-Elderly MVPF in \textcite{hendren2020unified}. The values of \(\alpha\) and \(\lambda\) are illustrative. The lesson depends on the location of the \(G\to0\) boundary, not on the precise level of the MVPF.} A more negative mortality effect raises measured willingness to pay and lowers net government cost.

The mechanical matched-control exercise is much more dramatic here. TWFE with controls gives \(\hat\beta=-53.21\), so \(G=0.202\) and the MVPF is \(8.91\). The corresponding CS-DR estimates that force the paper's time-varying controls into \texttt{xformla} are \(-6.78\) in the static aggregation and \(+7.46\) in the dynamic aggregation, implying MVPFs of \(1.23\) and \(0.80\). The mechanical span crosses the \(\mathrm{MVPF}=1\) benchmark.

That crossing is not the substantive finding. The audit classifies the CS-DR-with-controls estimates as structurally non-informative for this paper. The original design relies on high-dimensional interaction fixed effects, including urbanisation\(\times\)year and state\(\times\)year components, that cannot be reproduced by the CS propensity-score framework through \texttt{xformla}. The large standard errors on the CS-DR-with-controls estimates, \(27.7\) and \(45.4\), are a symptom of that mismatch. The dynamic positive point estimate is therefore a precision artefact, not evidence that CHCs increased elderly mortality.

The audit-filtered values remain in the Laffer region. TWFE without controls is \(-53.25\), which implies \(G=0.201\) and an MVPF of \(8.94\). The preferred CS comparison that uses the urbanisation proxy \(Durb_f\) gives \(-58.99\) in the group aggregation, \(-61.43\) in the simple aggregation, and \(-58.99\) in the dynamic aggregation. These imply MVPFs of \(16.37\), \(24.45\), and \(16.38\). A diagnostic rerun with no CS covariates at all gives \(-60.91\), \(-64.03\), and \(-63.96\), implying MVPFs of \(22.16\), \(49.61\), and \(48.34\).\footnote{The labels matter. The bundled main CS columns for this article use the \(Durb_f\) urbanisation proxy, not a no-covariate CS specification. The no-covariate values reported here come from a separate diagnostic rerun with \texttt{cs\_controls=[]}. Both versions place the estimate near the \(G\to0\) boundary.} The level of the MVPF is calibration-dependent, but the local geometry is not: once \(\hat\beta\) is near \(-60\), small movements in the mortality estimate produce large movements in welfare because \(G\) is close to zero.

The lesson from this case is about the welfare mapping, not a literal reversal of the CHC recommendation. The credible estimates are all negative and all place the policy in the Laffer region under this calibration. What changes is the intensity of the conclusion. Mortality-driven programs can sit close to the point where net fiscal cost approaches zero; in that region, even modest coefficient differences among credible estimates move the MVPF sharply.

\\subsubsection{Implication}

The two papers do not contradict each other. They are two ends of the same spectrum, and the spectrum is the curvature of the welfare mapping near the coefficient. Dranove's drug-price channel sits in a flat segment because the affected expenditure is a small share of total program cost. Bailey and Goodman-Bacon's elderly-mortality channel sits near a vertical asymptote because each averted death enters the fiscal channel directly in this calibration. The same kind of coefficient movement that leaves Dranove near \(MVPF=1\) can move Bailey by many MVPF units.

Two implications follow. The first is that magnitude instability in \(\hat\beta\) does not by itself imply welfare instability. Papers whose fiscal-externality channel sits far from the Laffer boundary tolerate substantial magnitude shifts without changing the recommendation. Papers whose channel sits near the boundary do not. In those cases, Lessons 1--6 need the most careful welfare reading because the fiscal mapping amplifies whatever aggregation, control-group, or covariate margin is active in \(\hat\beta\).

The second implication is that MVPF sensitivity is informative only after the underlying estimates have been audited. The mechanical table is useful for showing the shape of the welfare curve, but the welfare conclusion comes from the estimates that survive the audit. For Dranove, the audit-filtered estimates all imply MVPFs close to one. For Bailey and Goodman-Bacon, the mechanically matched CS estimates are discarded, but the credible TWFE, \(Durb_f\)-proxy CS, and no-covariate CS estimates all sit near the \(G\to0\) boundary and imply high MVPFs. A welfare analysis that reads \(\hat\beta\) off a consolidated table without first checking which estimates are implementationally well-formed can either overstate fragility, by treating non-informative estimates as substantive, or understate fragility, by missing that a credible coefficient range lies on a steep fiscal-externality curve. The pre-submission checklist in Box~4.1 folds this into its reporting list by asking papers to report their fiscal-externality channel alongside their preferred \(\hat\beta\).

\\subsection{A pre-submission checklist and a design-triage decision tree}
\label{sec:applied_guide}

The reanalysis produces two practical artefacts. Box~4.1 is a pre-submission checklist that asks an applied DiD paper to state the design choices the preceding lessons identify as empirically consequential. Figure~\ref{fig:decision_tree} is a design-triage decision tree. For each of four branching design margins it names a diagnostic, pairs it with a threshold anchored to the reanalysis, and states the specification rule the threshold triggers, and consolidates the implementation requirements that apply on every branch in a reporting block. \textcite{baker2025difference} provide the broader forward-design framework, from target parameter to assumptions to estimator to robustness. Our contribution is narrower. We use the reanalysis to isolate the empirical margins on which published results become harder to interpret under modern re-estimation. These two artefacts are not a complete design framework. They are most useful when paired with the forward-design logic of \textcite{baker2025difference} and with context-specific judgment about the identifying assumption. The goal is practical. We want applied researchers and referees to surface the margins the reanalysis identifies as consequential, before those margins are buried inside software defaults or left implicit in the event-study figure.

Box~4.1 is a reporting device. It helps the reader see where the design may still carry unspoken assumptions. Our reanalysis shows that disagreement with modern re-estimation concentrates in these margins. The recurring problem is not a generic failure of TWFE or a mechanical gain from switching estimators. The recurring problem is that the causal target, the identifying comparison, and the available support drift apart in ways the paper does not make explicit.

Figure~\ref{fig:decision_tree} walks through four branching design margins in sequence, covering estimator choice, control group, covariates, and dynamic support, and consolidates implementation requirements in a reporting block at the bottom. Each branching margin pairs a diagnostic with a threshold anchored to the reanalysis, and the threshold triggers a specification rule. The figure is framed as design triage, not a general estimator-choice recipe. The output is a primary specification that can be defended against the design questions the preceding lessons identified as consequential.

The applied lesson of the reanalysis is narrower than a full design framework, and it is close to what applied work needs. Papers become easier to interpret when they state the estimand before the coefficient, justify the control group as part of identification, treat the event study as a design diagnostic, explain the role of covariates, and make implementation choices visible. The central question is whether the empirical design keeps the causal target, the identifying comparison, and the available support aligned. When those objects drift apart, changing estimators will change the result without solving the underlying design problem.

\vspace{0.3em}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.6em,itemsep=0.25em,label=\arabic*.,start=10]
\item Is the panel balancing rule stated (full balanced, unbalanced, or pair-balanced)? \textit{See Lesson~6.}

\item Is the base-period convention (varying $t-1$ vs.\ universal $g-1$) documented? \textit{See Lesson~6.}

\item Is the fill rate reported for each panel article with non-trivial missingness?
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{E. Honest framing and comparability}

\vspace{0.3em}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.6em,itemsep=0.25em,label=\arabic*.,start=13]
\item Is the negative-weights diagnostic reported (share, Bacon decomposition) rather than treated as a verdict on TWFE validity? \textit{See Lesson~2.}

\item Is robustness to treatment-effect heterogeneity (estimator choice) distinguished from robustness to parallel-trends violations (HonestDiD)? \textit{See Lesson~5.}

\item Are the design-margin assumptions, including control group, covariate set, aggregation rule, balancing rule, and base period, visible to the reader rather than hidden inside software defaults? \textit{See Lessons~2, 3, and 6.}
\end{enumerate}

\\section{Conclusion}
\label{Chapter5}


Health-policy decisions often turn on the magnitude of a DiD estimate. Sign preservation does not settle the decision when the relevant threshold lies between two competing estimates. This dissertation asked how stable those magnitudes are under modern DiD estimators. It reassessed 53 health-economics studies under a common replication and re-estimation protocol. The literature does not collapse. Most results remain directionally recognizable, but many become less secure once the identifying comparison is made explicit. Instability in this sample appears less often as sign reversal and more often as shifts in magnitude, weaker dynamic profiles, loss of precision, and greater sensitivity to design choices than the original articles suggested.

The contribution is empirical and comparative. Re-estimating a defined applied literature under a common protocol separates results that remain stable from results that weaken once the design is interrogated along the margins modern DiD estimators force into view. That distinction reframes how the recent critique of TWFE should be read in practice. Disagreement in this sample is not driven mainly by negative weights. It more often reflects control-group choice, the role of covariates, aggregation, support at long horizons, endpoint binning, and implementation. The practical implication is that TWFE should not remain the default primary estimator in applied DiD. Modern estimators address heterogeneous-treatment-effect bias and also make the causal contrast transparent, exposing design choices that TWFE compresses inside a single coefficient.

The applied output is condensed into two artefacts at the end of Section~\ref{sec:applied_guide}. Box~4.1 is a fifteen-item pre-submission checklist anchored to the empirical patterns documented in the reanalysis. Figure~\ref{fig:decision_tree} is a design-triage guide for choosing the primary modern specification and for deciding which sensitivity margins belong in the main text. Together they translate the reanalysis into a workflow that helps researchers state the estimand, defend the untreated comparison, treat the event study as a design diagnostic, and make implementation choices visible before the result is presented as causal evidence. They complement design reasoning rather than replace it.

The exercise has clear limits. The sample is restricted to studies with usable replication materials and does not represent the full DiD literature. The analysis depends on the data and code environments provided by the original authors and inherits limitations from those replication packages. Coverage is concentrated in health economics and skewed toward studies set in the United States, which constrains generalization across fields and institutional settings. Several replications required adaptations to make the original specifications compatible with a standardized implementation in R. Modern estimators have their own limits. In designs with weak overlap, small samples, or high-dimensional covariates, they clarify the estimand at the cost of precision. The dissertation's claim is correspondingly narrow. It delivers an auditable and comparable reassessment of a defined literature, not a census of DiD applications.

These limits point to natural extensions. The core problem identified in this dissertation is not specific to TWFE in the canonical staggered binary case. It is the broader question of whether the empirical implementation keeps the causal target, the identifying comparison, and the available support aligned. Richer treatment settings make that alignment harder to see. Three extensions follow directly.

The first is triple-difference designs. Applying a comparable protocol to health settings with eligibility or geographic partitions, such as Medicaid expansions interacted with income bands or programs that vary across states and cohorts, would carry the decomposition by design margin into a richer identifying structure.

The second is non-absorbing treatments. \textcite{dechaisemartin2020twfe} handle policy on-and-off dynamics that the current sample excludes by construction. Those estimators are the natural instrument for revisiting health-policy studies with reversible treatment, such as temporary Medicaid expansions or short-duration mandates.

The third is designs with compositional change. \textcite{sant2026difference} accommodate shifts in the composition of treated units over time, a setting common to health-policy studies where eligibility rules evolve during the study window.

The reanalysis of a literature is a pointed intervention. What this exercise leaves behind is a more demanding discipline of design justification, auditable, comparable, and portable to any DiD literature willing to reexecute the question.
