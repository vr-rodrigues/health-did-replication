\begin{abstract}
\noindent Two-way fixed-effects (TWFE) difference-in-differences has been the
default estimator in applied health-policy research for decades, yet recent
theory shows it can implicitly weight cohort--time effects in ways that conflict
with the causal parameter. We ask whether the conclusions of 56 published
health-economics papers that used TWFE survive reestimation with modern
heterogeneity-robust estimators (Callaway--Sant'Anna, Sun--Abraham,
Borusyak--Jaravel--Spiess, Goodman-Bacon, and the Rambachan--Roth HonestDiD
sensitivity framework). We replace only the estimator and keep each paper's
original specification. The sign of the effect is preserved in 48 of the 56
cases and reverses in 8. The costs of legacy TWFE designs in
this literature are concentrated in precision and magnitude rather than in
directional conclusions: standard errors rise by roughly 42\% on average, 12
articles lose conventional statistical significance, and the point estimate
moves by more than 20\% in the majority of staggered-adoption designs. Bacon
decompositions show that treated-versus-treated comparisons account for a
substantial share of the identifying variation under staggered TWFE, consistent
with the mechanism emphasized by the theory literature. HonestDiD sensitivity
further shows that robustness to parallel-trends violations is limited under
both TWFE and CS-DID. We release a reproducible metadata-driven pipeline and
the regenerated results for the 56 papers under CC-BY-4.0, compliant with the
AEA Data and Code Availability Policy 1.0.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} difference-in-differences, TWFE, heterogeneous
treatment effects, Callaway--Sant'Anna, Sun--Abraham, Borusyak--Jaravel--Spiess,
HonestDiD, replication, health economics.

\smallskip
\noindent\textbf{JEL codes:} C21, C23, I18.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{Chapter1}

Two-way fixed-effects (TWFE) has been the default estimator for difference-in-differences (DiD) in applied health economics for decades. Over the past five years, the methodological literature has shown that TWFE can produce estimates that look like causal parameters but combine cohort-time contrasts in ways the researcher did not intend \parencite{goodman2021difference,sun2021estimating,callaway2021difference,roth2023s}. The question this paper asks is narrow and empirical: \textit{when we replace TWFE with a modern heterogeneity-robust estimator in published health-economics DiD studies, how often --- and on what margin --- do the conclusions change?}

We re-estimate the headline result of 56 published health-economics DiD studies under a standardized modern-estimator protocol. After excluding 3 articles whose published specifications cannot be reproduced under our standardized template (\textcite{myers2017power}, \textcite{moorthy2025bluecollar}, and \textcite{kuziemko2018managed}; see Appendix~\ref{AppendixD}), the comparable subsample contains 53 articles. The sign of the effect is preserved in 47 of the 53 cases and reverses in 6. Statistical significance is lost in 14 of the originally significant estimates, and the standard error rises by a median of 36\%. The within-paper proportional shift in the point estimate ranges from $-431\%$ in \textcite{maclean2025paid} to $+542\%$ in \textcite{levine2011effective}. Modern DiD estimators rarely overturn published health-policy conclusions. They routinely weaken them.

This headline conceals substantial within-paper heterogeneity. 32\% of estimates shift by more than 50\% in magnitude, and 21\% shift by more than 100\% (see Figure~\ref{fig:ch3_aggregate_variation}, Section~\ref{Chapter4}). The width of that distribution is the empirical substance of the exercise. When a policy's cost-benefit ratio depends on the size of the treatment effect --- the typical case in health --- a 50\% or 100\% magnitude shift can flip the adoption decision even when the sign does not move. The relevant empirical margin is magnitude, not sign alone.

The contribution of this paper is to identify \textit{which} design margins account for the TWFE-vs-modern gap in this literature. Three findings reframe the applied debate.

First, the negative-weights critique that has organized methodological attention since \textcite{goodman2021difference} is empirically second-order. In our staggered subsample, the mean share of negative weights is \textbf{6.4\%} and the median is \textbf{0.4\%} (Table~\ref{tab:bacon_summary}). These are smaller numbers than the popular mental model would suggest. The dominant sources of divergence lie elsewhere: control-group choice (Lesson~6), covariate handling (Lesson~7, where 33 of 36 originally-controlled papers compare TWFE-with-covariates to unconditional CS-DID), dynamic aggregation (Lesson~9, where 12 of 52 papers change classification across aggregation rules), endpoint binning (Lesson~4, with a median $|\Delta| = 34\%$ under progressive binning across 21 papers), and software defaults (Lesson~10).

Second, robustness to treatment-effect heterogeneity does not imply robustness to parallel-trends violations. The median HonestDiD breakdown value \parencite{rambachan2023more} at the first post-treatment period is zero under both TWFE and CS-DID (Lesson~5). Switching to a heterogeneity-robust estimator sharpens the mapping from the coefficient to an estimand but does not rescue designs whose identifying counterfactual is itself fragile. These are two separate questions, and the applied literature still tends to conflate them.

Third, the ten empirical lessons in Section~\ref{Chapter4} condense into a one-page pre-submission checklist (Box~4.1 in Section~\ref{sec:applied_guide}) whose items are calibrated to the empirical prevalence of each failure mode in this sample. This checklist is the paper's intended practical deliverable and is complementary, not alternative, to the conceptual framework of \textcite{baker2025difference}.

Compared to prior reanalysis exercises in finance and accounting \parencite{baker2022much}, political science \parencite{chiu2023and}, and cross-field reproducibility \parencite{brodeur2026reproducibility}, this paper goes deeper rather than wider. Those studies establish the \emph{prevalence} of TWFE-vs-modern divergence across many fields; we \emph{decompose} the divergence within one field, isolating the contribution of five design margins --- binning, geographic conditioning, control-group choice, covariate handling, and software defaults --- that the prior literature treats as secondary to the negative-weights critique. To our knowledge, this is the first applied reanalysis to systematically document the joint contribution of these margins in any published DiD literature, and the only one to report that the negative-weights share itself is empirically second-order (mean $0.064$, Table~\ref{tab:bacon_summary}) once the larger design margins are surfaced. Health economics is a natural site for this decomposition: DiD is the workhorse identification design in the subfield \parencite{goldsmith2024tracking} and the policy stakes are unusually explicit, because welfare and cost-effectiveness calculations depend directly on the magnitude of the estimated treatment effect.

The rest of the paper is organized as follows. Section~\ref{Chapter2} reviews the methodological and reanalysis literatures that motivate the exercise. Section~\ref{Chapter3} describes the 56-article sample, the standardized re-estimation protocol, the identification assumptions that discipline the external validity of our meta-statistic (Section~\ref{sec:identification}), and the AI-assisted workflow. Section~\ref{Chapter4} presents ten lessons organized around the empirical mechanisms behind TWFE-vs-modern divergence (§§4.1--4.10), culminating in the pre-submission checklist (§\ref{sec:applied_guide}). Section~\ref{Chapter5} concludes. Per-article results, HonestDiD sensitivity analyses, and Bacon decompositions are documented in Appendix~\ref{AppendixB}; the AI-assisted pipeline in Appendix~\ref{AppendixC}; the sample-construction log in Appendix~\ref{AppendixD}.


\section{Related Literature}
\label{Chapter2}

\subsection{Classical TWFE approach and its problems}

Recent work on DiD begins from a now well-established point. With multiple periods, staggered adoption, and heterogeneous treatment effects, the \emph{two-way fixed effects} (TWFE) estimator no longer admits the canonical \(2\times 2\) interpretation. In its most common form,
\begin{equation}
Y_{it} = \alpha_i + \lambda_t + \beta D_{it} + \varepsilon_{it},
\label{eq:twfe_static}
\end{equation}
where \(Y_{it}\) is the outcome of interest, \(\alpha_i\) and \(\lambda_t\) are unit and period fixed effects, and \(D_{it}\) indicates treatment. In the \(2\times 2\) case, under parallel trends and no anticipation, \(\beta\) coincides with the usual DiD estimand. In staggered settings, that interpretation breaks down. \textcite{roth2023s} synthesize the theoretical literature and argue that the main insight is not only about weighting. It also concerns which comparisons identify the effect, how dynamic specifications should be interpreted, and how covariates enter the design. The exposition below follows that three-part division.

\subsubsection{Weighting and the Goodman-Bacon decomposition}

\textcite{goodman2021difference} shows that, in the canonical staggered-adoption TWFE design without covariates, the coefficient can be written as a weighted average of admissible \(2\times 2\) DiD estimators:
\begin{equation}
\hat{\beta}^{TWFE}
=
\sum_{j} \omega_j \hat{\beta}^{2\times 2}_j,
\qquad
\sum_j \omega_j = 1.
\label{eq:bacon_generic_short}
\end{equation}
The main implication is that TWFE combines multiple cohort-time comparisons rather than recovering a single ATT-type parameter.\footnote{In \textcite{goodman2021difference}, the relevant comparisons include treated-versus-never-treated and earlier-treated-versus-later-treated \(2\times 2\) designs. The notation \(\omega_j\) in equation~\eqref{eq:bacon_generic_short} is used here as a compact generic label for the Goodman-Bacon weights. A related insight appears in \textcite{dechaisemartin2020twfe}, who study a broader two-way fixed-effects setting and show that the TWFE estimand can be expressed as a weighted sum of group-period treatment effects with weights that sum to one but may be negative.} Once treatment effects vary across cohorts or over exposure time, some of those comparisons become difficult to interpret as clean causal contrasts. The coefficient may then depart from the effect researchers intend to estimate, sometimes by a great deal. The applied consequence is that a single TWFE coefficient can conceal considerable internal heterogeneity across the $2\times 2$ contrasts that construct it.

\subsubsection{Dynamic specifications and event-study contamination}

A parallel problem arises in dynamic \emph{event-study} specifications:
\begin{equation}
Y_{it}
=
\alpha_i + \lambda_t
+ \sum_{\ell \neq -1} \mu_{\ell}\mathbf{1}\{t-E_i=\ell\}
+ u_{it},
\label{eq:twfe_event_study_short}
\end{equation}
where \(E_i\) denotes the treatment adoption period and \(\ell=t-E_i\) is event time. A common interpretation is that \(\mu_\ell\) measures the average treatment effect at horizon \(\ell\). \textcite{sun2021estimating} show that this interpretation generally breaks down under staggered adoption with heterogeneous treatment effects across cohorts: the coefficient for horizon \(\ell\) generally loads not only on the effect at that horizon but also on effects from other relative periods. Their interaction-weighted estimator removes this contamination by averaging cohort-specific effects only across cohorts that identify the same horizon. This matters because applied work routinely uses lead coefficients to assess pre-trends and lag coefficients to describe timing and persistence. Under treatment-effect heterogeneity, those coefficients may misstate both the credibility of the design and the shape of the treatment profile they are supposed to recover.

\subsubsection{Modern DiD estimators: identification before aggregation}

Recent work has reoriented the discussion toward designs that identify treatment effects at the group-time level \emph{before} any aggregation. In \textcite{callaway2021difference}, the basic object is the group-time average treatment effect,
\begin{equation}
ATT(g,t)
=
\mathbb{E}\!\left[ Y_t(g)-Y_t(0)\mid G_g=1 \right],
\label{eq:att_gt_short}
\end{equation}
where \(G_g\) denotes the group first treated in period \(g\). The key contribution lies in the identification strategy: treatment effects are first defined for each \((g,t)\) cell and only afterward aggregated into summary parameters. This makes the comparison group explicit. In practice, the choice appears in the distinction between \emph{never-treated} and \emph{not-yet-treated} controls, which correspond to different versions of the parallel trends assumption. \textcite{marcus2021role} formalize the trade-off between these two comparison groups in event-study settings: the not-yet-treated group is typically larger and more efficient but relies on a stronger parallel-trends assumption (pre-trends must hold for later cohorts in their untreated years), while the never-treated group imposes a weaker assumption but at the cost of efficiency when the untreated pool is thin. \textcite{callaway2021difference} implements both versions, leaving the choice to the applied researcher as a substantive design decision rather than a technical default.

Complementary estimators build on the same principle of identifying $ATT(g,t)$ first. \textcite{borusyak2024revisiting} propose an imputation approach that explicitly models untreated outcomes and infers treatment effects by extrapolation, which \textcite{gardner2022two} implements as a two-stage estimator. \textcite{wooldridge2021two,wooldridge2025two} shows that a saturated version of TWFE --- his Extended Two-Way Fixed Effects (ETWFE) --- recovers the $ATT(g,t)$ targets of Callaway--Sant'Anna through a standard regression with interaction terms. \textcite{arkhangelsky2021synthetic} introduce Synthetic Difference-in-Differences, which combines synthetic-control re-weighting with DiD, and has since become a further alternative. We do not use Synthetic DiD in our reanalysis, but mention it here because it forms part of the modern methodological landscape the applied researcher now faces. The common thread across these estimators is that they make explicit which comparisons identify the effect and at which level aggregation occurs; they differ in how they construct the counterfactual and in what assumptions identification requires.

\subsubsection{Parallel trends: sensitivity, not pre-testing}

Even with more interpretable estimators, identification still depends on some version of parallel trends. Applied work often evaluates that assumption through pre-trend tests in dynamic specifications. \textcite{roth2022pretest} shows why this practice is weaker than it often appears: failure to reject pre-trends may simply reflect low power, and conditioning the analysis on passing that test induces pre-test bias. \textcite{ghanem2024selection} further clarify when parallel trends is itself defensible, linking the assumption to selection models and showing that it holds only under specific restrictions on unobserved heterogeneity.

\textcite{rambachan2023more} propose a different approach, replacing the pass-or-fail logic of pre-trend tests with explicit sensitivity analysis. Rather than treat exact parallel trends as something established by insignificant leads, they ask how conclusions change under bounded deviations from that benchmark. This shift is important for the broader literature because it moves credibility assessment away from mechanical hypothesis testing and toward transparent robustness to plausible violations. In the applied reanalysis below, we implement the Rambachan--Roth sensitivity framework on every article that has a dynamic event study, alongside the estimator-swap exercise.

\subsubsection{Covariates as part of the design}

Covariate choices are also part of identification. In modern DiD designs, adding controls replaces an unconditional parallel trends assumption with a conditional one, so the design begins to depend on which covariates enter and how they enter. In \textcite{callaway2021difference}, this conditioning is built around pre-treatment covariates, and \(ATT(g,t)\) can be implemented through outcome regression, inverse probability weighting, or doubly robust estimands --- the last of which is the \textcite{sant2020doubly} estimator whose efficiency gains hold even when parallel trends is unconditional. This is conceptually different from the familiar practice of adding controls to a TWFE regression and interpreting the result as a more flexible version of the same estimand.

The distinction becomes sharper once one considers time-varying covariates. As emphasized by \textcite{caetano2022difference}, if treatment can affect these covariates, conditioning on their realized post-treatment values may amount to conditioning on mediators or selection variables rather than on untreated characteristics. Even when treatment does not affect them directly, \textcite{caetano_callaway2024_hiddenlinearity} and \textcite{wooldridge2025two} show that standard TWFE implementations can still impose additional and often opaque restrictions. After removing unit fixed effects, the regression may condition only on changes in time-varying covariates, while time-invariant covariates disappear from the transformed specification altogether. The lesson from this literature is that conditioning choices alter the maintained assumptions behind the design and can affect interpretation in ways that are easy to miss in routine empirical practice.

\subsubsection{Implementation as a finite-sample concern}

Implementation details can also matter in finite samples. In principle, differences across modern DiD estimators should reflect differences in identification, aggregation, or target parameters. In practice, numerical choices such as balancing rules, support restrictions, and first-step estimation can also affect results, especially in highly unbalanced panels or designs with weak overlap. This point belongs in the literature review because it clarifies that disagreements across estimates need not always reflect purely conceptual differences: some arise because distinct commands operationalize the same estimand differently in finite samples. Primary evidence for this claim appears in Section~\ref{sec:li_sensitivity} (Lesson~10), which documents three balancing rules under a nominally identical CS-DID specification producing qualitatively different post-treatment profiles in one of the reanalyzed studies.

\subsection{The reanalysis movement in economics and adjacent fields}

This paper sits inside a broader disciplinary movement to re-examine published findings using more credible tools. The psychology replication crisis that crystallized with the \textcite{openscience2015reproducibility} and \textcite{camerer2018evaluating} reproducibility projects has reshaped how applied economists think about the empirical record: credibility of a finding is assessed not only at the point of first publication, but over a longer horizon that includes replication, re-estimation under alternative methods, and generalization to adjacent settings \parencite{vivalt2020specification}. The present exercise inherits that framing. The contribution of this paper is not to argue for the credibility of any individual DiD estimate; it is to ask how the modal DiD estimate in a defined applied literature moves when the estimator that originally produced it is replaced.

Within economics, three recent reanalysis studies define the immediate predecessors of this exercise. Rather than review them chronologically, we organize them by the distinct contribution each makes to the present paper.

\textbf{\textcite{baker2022much}} --- \textit{applied consequence demonstration.} BLW connect the theoretical critique of staggered TWFE to applications in corporate finance and accounting. Their contribution is less about the scale of the exercise than about establishing, in a defined applied literature, that replacing TWFE with heterogeneity-robust estimators can change magnitudes, dynamic profiles, and specification choices such as endpoint binning. The value for us is methodological: BLW shows that the estimator-swap exercise is a productive unit of analysis, and their diagnostic framework for staggered-adoption finance designs transfers directly to our setting.

\textbf{\textcite{chiu2023and}} --- \textit{identification-as-fragility insight.} Chiu, Lau, Lau, and Xu reanalyze 49 studies published in the \emph{American Political Science Review}, \emph{American Journal of Political Science}, and \emph{The Journal of Politics} that use TWFE for causal inference in observational panel settings. Their contribution reshapes the reanalysis agenda. In many applications, heterogeneity-robust estimators remain qualitatively close to TWFE; some show sign reversals or precision losses, but those are not the modal outcome. The more consequential finding concerns identification itself: only a subset of studies produces direct evidence against parallel trends, but many more are too underpowered to rule out violations large enough to alter the practical meaning of the estimate. Chiu et al.\ thereby shift the emphasis of reanalysis from \emph{weighting correction} to \emph{identification credibility} --- a shift we inherit and extend via the HonestDiD sensitivity analysis in Lesson~5.

\textbf{\textcite{brodeur2026reproducibility}} --- \textit{robustness-vs-reproducibility distinction.} Brodeur and co-authors reanalyze 110 recent papers published in top economics and political-science journals under mandatory data and code sharing policies. Their contribution is taxonomic: they distinguish \emph{computational reproducibility} (can the published numbers be recovered from the supplied code?) from \emph{robustness} (do the substantive conclusions survive alternative but reasonable analytical choices?). Reproducibility rates are high, but robustness rates are substantially lower, and the gap is a diagnostic about where the literature is most fragile. For the present paper, that distinction is clarifying: our exercise is a robustness exercise, not a reproducibility one. We do not primarily test whether each paper's original code recovers its published numbers; we test whether its conclusions survive re-estimation with estimators developed after its publication.

\subsubsection{The gap this paper fills}
\label{sec:ch2_gap}

\textcite{baker2022much} covers corporate finance and accounting. \textcite{chiu2023and} covers political science. \textcite{brodeur2026reproducibility} covers cross-field robustness. \textbf{None of them, and no comparable study we are aware of, examines health economics at scale or systematically documents the contribution of specific design margins --- binning, geographic conditioning, dynamic aggregation, covariate handling, and software defaults --- to the apparent divergence between TWFE and modern estimators in any single applied subfield.} This is the gap the paper fills.

Health economics is a natural site for this exercise for three reasons. First, DiD is the workhorse empirical design in the subfield and its use has accelerated over the past decade \parencite{goldsmith2024tracking}. Second, the policy stakes are unusually explicit: many of the re-estimated articles inform concrete welfare or cost-effectiveness calculations in which the magnitude of the treatment effect, not only its sign, determines whether the policy clears the adoption threshold (see Section~\ref{Chapter4}, Lesson~1). Third, the health-econ literature has developed its own applied best-practice guidance for DiD \parencite{wing2018designing}, which means there is an established methodological anchor against which our reanalysis findings can be read. The three predecessors named above do not provide that anchor for health.

This paper therefore trades breadth across fields for depth across design margins within a single field. Rather than replicate the strategy of examining estimator swaps only, we document \emph{how} the swap changes the estimate --- which margin of the design carries the change, and whether the change reflects genuine correction, estimand redefinition, or implementation artefact. The ten lessons in Section~\ref{Chapter4} are organized around those margins.

\subsection{Sample, protocol, and scope}

The reanalysis draws on \textit{health economics} articles published between 2000 and 2025 in 17 general-interest and field journals. From 8,171 candidate articles retrieved from OpenAlex, an LLM-based screen of abstracts flags 1,884 as likely \emph{DiD} studies. We then search for replication materials, consolidate records across sources, deduplicate by DOI, and manually review 431 located cases for eligibility. The final sample contains 56 articles that use \emph{DiD} as the main empirical strategy and provide a full replication package, allowing the main specification to be re-estimated under a common protocol. Table~\ref{tab:sample_pipeline} summarizes the selection process and Appendix~\ref{AppendixD} documents each stage of the collection pipeline in detail.

Of the 56 reanalyzed articles, 32 use staggered adoption and 24 one-time adoption. Thirty-eight use panel data and 18 use \emph{repeated cross-sections} (RCS), and 50 have an estimable dynamic \emph{event study}. Table~\ref{tab:sample_detailed} cross-tabulates adoption type and data structure. These dimensions determine estimator coverage and the construction of the counterfactual. The sample covers topics such as vaccination, insurance regulation, opioids, pollution, maternal and child health, tax credits, and epidemics. Table~\ref{tab:journal_final} reports the journal distribution, and Appendix~\ref{AppendixB} lists the reanalyzed studies.

The unit of analysis is the article's main specification. For each study, we preserve the central empirical result and re-estimate it with alternative econometric tools. Doing so requires a consistent rule to select one outcome and one main specification per article, while avoiding case-by-case discretion. Selection follows a simple hierarchy. We first prioritize specifications explicitly described by the authors as main, preferred, or baseline. If more than one candidate remains, we choose the one estimated on the largest number of observations. In the event of a tie, we apply three tie-breakers, in this order: preference for the most aggregated outcome variable (e.g., all-cause mortality over cause-specific categories), then for specifications with fewer time-varying covariates, and, only as a last resort, a documented random selection. When the article reports only a dynamic event study, with no directly comparable static ATT, we re-estimate a collapsed version with a single post-treatment indicator while preserving the sample, controls, fixed effects, and clustering level of the original study. All decisions are recorded in an auditable log described in Appendix~\ref{AppendixA}.

\subsection{Identification and scope of the meta-statistic}
\label{sec:identification}

\noindent\textbf{Identification.} The central meta-statistic of the paper --- the share of the 56 reanalyzed articles whose original TWFE conclusion is preserved under the standardized modern-estimator protocol --- targets the rate at which a representative published health-economics DiD study would survive modern re-estimation. This interpretation rests on two maintained assumptions.

\textit{Representativeness of the 56-paper sample.} The 56 reanalyzed articles are assumed to be representative of the larger population of 1,884 DiD candidate articles identified at Step~2 of the sample construction pipeline (Table~\ref{tab:sample_pipeline}) along the design margins that drive TWFE-vs-modern divergence: control-group choice, covariate set, dynamic aggregation rule, endpoint binning, geographic conditioning, and software defaults. This assumption is partially testable. Table~\ref{tab:selection_balance} reports observable-characteristic balance between the reanalyzed sample and a 537-article DiD-classified pool retained during sample construction, on publication year and journal tier. The reanalyzed sample trends slightly more recent (mean 2019.9 vs 2018.4, Welch $p=0.010$) and is heavily over-represented in AEJ-family journals (50.9\% vs 2.5\%) and in \emph{Review of Economics and Statistics} (18.9\% vs 1.0\%), while under-represented in field journals (24.5\% vs 95.2\%; all $p<0.001$). This concentration reflects the data-and-code policies of these venues and confirms that the reanalyzed sample is not a random draw from the DiD literature: it is the ``replication-package-available subset,'' skewed toward publication venues with strict reproducibility requirements. Because the 97\% attrition from 1,884 to 56 is concentrated at Step~3 (location and screening of replication materials), the selection effect plausibly biases the headline numbers toward the more careful end of the published literature. Where this margin matters for interpretation, we flag it explicitly in the text and, in Section~\ref{Chapter5}, discuss how robust the qualitative conclusions are to that bias.

\input{Tables/table_3_selection_balance.tex}

\textit{Estimator recovery by the reanalysis protocol.} Conditional on each article's declared specification and the design margins above, the AI-assisted reestimation protocol is assumed to recover the modern estimator that the original authors would have selected had they worked from the modern-DiD toolkit available in 2026. This assumption is not formally testable within this study. The protocol is deterministic for a fixed random seed, and the eleven held-out calibration articles (Appendix~\ref{AppendixC}) match the hand-coded benchmark exactly. That validation speaks to execution fidelity but not to the counterfactual estimator choice the original authors themselves would have debated had they rewritten the paper in 2026.

If either assumption fails in a direction correlated with the survival outcome, the headline meta-statistic should be read as an internal descriptive fact about the 56-article sample rather than as a population parameter. Throughout the paper, we frame our findings as diagnoses of the 56 studies we actually re-estimated and are explicit when we extrapolate more broadly.

\subsubsection{Selection bounds on the population preservation rate}
\label{sec:selection_bounds}

Because representativeness is an untested assumption, we complement the point estimate with worst-case bounds in the style of \textcite{manski1989anatomy}. Of the 56 reanalyzed articles, 48 preserve the sign of the original TWFE conclusion under the modern-estimator protocol, a within-sample preservation rate of $\hat\pi_1 = 48/56 = 85.7\%$. Let $\pi_0$ denote the (unobserved) preservation rate among the $N_0 = 1{,}828$ articles excluded during sample construction. The population-level preservation rate is a weighted average,
\[
\pi = \frac{N_1 \hat\pi_1 + N_0 \pi_0}{N_1 + N_0} = \frac{47 + 1{,}831\,\pi_0}{1{,}884},
\]
where $N_1 = 53$ is the comparable subsample (the 56 reanalyzed articles minus the three structural exclusions). Without further assumptions, $\pi_0 \in [0, 1]$ implies the uninformative Manski bound $\pi \in [2.5\%, 99.8\%]$.

Tightening the bound requires an assumption about the excluded population. Table~\ref{tab:selection_bounds} reports implied population rates under five scenarios for $\pi_0$. Under Manski--Pepper monotone selection --- the assumption that excluded articles have weakly lower replication-package quality and hence weakly lower preservation under modern re-estimation, so that $\pi_0 \leq \hat\pi_1$ --- the population rate satisfies $\pi \leq 88.7\%$. This is the directionally informative bound we take forward: the within-sample rate we report is an \emph{upper bound} on the population parameter, not an unbiased estimate.

\subsection{Estimators and coverage}

We re-estimate each article's main specification with different econometric tools. We reproduce the \emph{two-way fixed effects} (\emph{TWFE}) model as the baseline estimator, keeping the basic structure of the original article whenever possible. We do not audit the authors' code line by line. The goal is to reconstruct the original result in a standardized environment and compare it to modern estimators.

Our primary alternative is the estimator of \textcite{callaway2021difference} with dynamic agreggation. We use it because it accommodates treatment-effect heterogeneity under staggered adoption, makes the comparison group explicit, and allows versions with covariates and dynamic paths comparable to the \emph{event studies} used in the literature. Whenever the design allows, we estimate two CS-DID versions, one with a \emph{never-treated} comparison group and another with a \emph{not-yet-treated} group. We also estimate, when applicable, Sun and Abraham and Gardner (\texttt{did2s}).\footnote{The choice of \texttt{did2s} as an additional estimator mainly reflects operational convenience and broad compatibility with the designs observed in the sample, including \emph{repeated cross-sections}.} These estimators serve as cross-checks rather than replacements for the main benchmark. They indicate whether the main results survive different ways of handling heterogeneity, aggregation, and counterfactual construction.

Not all estimators are used in every part of the reanalysis, and not all articles have an estimable pre-treatment path. In this paper, the static benchmark is based on TWFE, CS-NT, and CS-NYT, with CS-DID serving as the main modern DiD reference. Gardner and Sun \& Abraham are used in the dynamic analysis as robustness checks for event-study profiles, following a methodological choice of this paper rather than a limitation of those estimators. Appendix~\ref{AppendixB} presents the individual results, event studies, and sensitivity analyses for each of the 56 articles. Table~\ref{tab:estimator_coverage} summarizes estimator coverage under this protocol.

\subsection{AI-assisted estimation and calibration protocol}

Reanalyzing 56 articles required a workflow that could handle heterogeneous replication packages without changing the estimation layer across studies. We built a semi-automated protocol adapted from the separation between substantive judgment and standardized execution emphasized by \textcite{xu2026scaling}. The workflow has three stages, illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:ai_workflow}. A profiling stage reads the article PDF and replication code to extract a structured specification file. An estimation stage feeds this specification into a fixed template that runs all applicable estimators, producing point estimates and event-study outputs. A reporting stage assembles the results into a standardized comparison with the original finding. The estimation template is the same for every article, and differences across studies enter only through the specification file and through documented preprocessing choices. Appendix~\ref{AppendixC} documents the full architecture, the metadata contract, and all pipeline stages in detail\footnote{The structured specification files, estimation scripts, outputs, and protocol-revision logs are available in the replication package.}.

We developed the protocol through a supervised calibration exercise based on 11 articles previously analyzed by hand. For each of these articles, the semi-automated workflow was asked to reconstruct the design from scratch under the standardized execution rules, without relying on the original hand-coded implementation. The resulting estimates were then compared against the manual benchmark. Any discrepancy triggered a diagnostic review, followed by revisions to the protocol until the automated reconstruction matched the benchmark sufficiently closely. This process produced a catalog of 28 recurring implementation problems and their corresponding resolution rules.\footnote{Examples include Stata date encodings, missing standard errors in plotting routines, silent dropping of horizons in event-study implementations, singular matrices in small-cohort CS-DID estimation, contamination of the never-treated comparison group by late-treated units, and differences between cumulative and marginal event-study aggregation across software. The full catalog is documented in Appendix~\ref{AppendixC}.} Once the workflow reliably reproduced the calibration set, it was extended to the remaining 45 articles in the sample. The researcher reviewed each specification before execution, inspected each event-study output, and approved all decisions involving sample restrictions, treatment timing, controls, and cross-estimator comparability. When an article required preprocessing beyond the default rules, that adjustment was encoded through standardized specification fields and documented for replication.\footnote{These cases included, for example, composite treatment indicators, time-varying treatment within units, and individual-level repeated cross-sections requiring aggregation.}

\subsection{Lesson 1. The literature does not collapse, but it weakens}

Replacing TWFE with modern DiD estimators does not overturn the earlier literature. Across the 53-article comparable subsample (three of the 56 reanalyzed articles --- \textcite{myers2017power}, \textcite{moorthy2025bluecollar}, and \textcite{kuziemko2018managed} --- are excluded from the headline comparison because of structural specification mismatches between the published spec and what our standardized template can reproduce; see Appendix~\ref{AppendixD}), the sign of the estimated effect is preserved in 47 cases and reverses in only 6. The main differences appear elsewhere. Losses of statistical significance are more common than gains, and changes in magnitude are often large. Table~\ref{tab:ch3_aggregate_concordance} summarizes this pattern. The empirical divergence between estimators comes less from reversing the direction of the result than from rescaling effects and weakening precision.

Figures~\ref{fig:ch3_aggregate_scatter} and \ref{fig:ch3_aggregate_variation} reinforce the same point. In the scatter plot, most articles remain close to the 45-degree line, which is consistent with the high rate of sign concordance. At the same time, the dispersion is far from trivial. The magnitude plot shows that many articles remain in a moderate range, but also that the tails are large enough to matter for interpretation.

This matters because the relevant empirical margin is often magnitude, not sign alone. In policy evaluation, the question is usually not just whether an estimated effect is positive or negative, but whether it is large enough to justify adoption once costs and budget constraints are taken into account. In health economics, this logic is often expressed in net-benefit terms, and the same reasoning extends more broadly to welfare analysis \parencite{johannesson1993decision,stinnett1998net,claxton1999irrelevance,hendren2020unified,finkelstein2020welfare}. A policy may remain beneficial only if the estimated gain clears a threshold set by cost-effectiveness or fiscal trade-offs.\footnote{Following \textcite{stinnett1998net}, the net health benefit is \(\text{NHB}(\lambda)=\Delta E-\Delta C/\lambda\), measured in health units, while the net monetary benefit is \(\text{NMB}(\lambda)=\lambda\Delta E-\Delta C\), measured in monetary units. Here \(\Delta E\) is the health gain, \(\Delta C\) is the policy cost, and \(\lambda\) is the social value of an additional unit of health. Both formulations imply the same adoption rule: the policy is adopted when \(\text{NHB}(\lambda)>0\), or equivalently when \(\Delta E>\Delta C/\lambda\). With estimated effects, the same logic applies to \(\widehat{\Delta E}_j\), where \(j\in\{\text{TWFE},\text{CS}\}\). Two estimates can have the same sign and still imply different decisions whenever the relevant threshold lies between them.} For that reason, a reanalysis that leaves the sign unchanged can still alter the policy conclusion. When 32.1\% of articles change by more than 50\% in magnitude and 20.8\% change by more than 100\%, an estimate may remain positive or negative in the same direction and still no longer justify the same decision.

\subsection{Lesson 2. Divergence is concentrated in staggered designs}

Divergence is concentrated in staggered-adoption designs. Part of the aggregate stability in the sample comes from articles with one-time adoption, where the gap between TWFE and estimators built for heterogeneous treatment effects is usually smaller. Once treatment timing varies across units, each adoption date defines a distinct treatment group, new control-group choices enter the design, and the final estimate becomes an aggregation across cohort--time effects. Under those conditions, disagreement across estimators becomes much more common, as Table~\ref{tab:ch3_timing_heterogeneity} shows clearly.

The source of this divergence is well understood by the modern DiD literature. In one-time adoption designs, the identifying comparison often remains close to a transparent treated-versus-untreated contrast. In staggered designs, however, the estimate is built from multiple cohort--time comparisons, which opens the door to treated-versus-treated comparisons, alternative control-group definitions, changing composition across event time, and implicit weighting across heterogeneous effects. Table~\ref{tab:bacon_summary} makes this concrete. The more consequential feature is not the prevalence of negative weights themselves but the share of identifying variation coming from treated-versus-treated comparisons: in the staggered subsample, this share averages 35\% and exceeds 50\% in 8 of 26 articles. Negative weights do appear in most articles, but their magnitude is typically modest (median 0.4\%, mean 6.4\%, maximum 48.5\%), so they rarely drive divergence on their own. Once the treated-versus-treated share is high, the TWFE coefficient no longer maps cleanly to a single causal contrast, and the link between the regression coefficient and the estimand of interest becomes weaker --- whether or not any individual comparison receives a negative weight. Staggered TWFE estimates are not always invalid. But when estimators diverge, the difference usually comes from the design itself: the control group, the weighting structure, and the aggregation rule. One consequence is asymmetric: divergence in the staggered subsample goes in both directions. Some articles move from originally-significant to insignificant under CS-DID, as is well documented in the literature, but a small number also move in the opposite direction --- from originally-insignificant to significant once the problematic treated-versus-treated comparisons are removed. Table~\ref{tab:ch3_timing_heterogeneity} reports this pattern. The practical message is that modern estimators do not mechanically shrink published findings; in a minority of designs they surface effects that TWFE had diluted.

\paragraph{The amplification quartet.} Four articles in the comparable subsample illustrate this surfacing-not-shrinking pattern in the same direction. \textcite{bancalari2024unintended}, where the headline TWFE on infant mortality is +0.74 (acknowledged in the paper as attenuated) while CS-DID is +1.79 --- a 2.4$\times$ larger magnitude, identical sign. \textcite{bhalotra2022maternal}, where TWFE estimates the reserved-seat-quota effect on log MMR at $-0.082$ while CS-DID gives $-0.127$ ($+55\%$ in absolute magnitude). \textcite{soliman2025disrupting}, where TWFE on opioid prescribing under DEA crackdowns is $-31.5$ while CS-DID, clipped to the same event window, is $-41.0$ ($+30\%$). \textcite{courtemanche2025chain}, where TWFE on calorie-posting laws is $-0.174$ while CS-DID is $-0.448$ ($2.5\times$). All four share the same Bacon decomposition signature --- a non-trivial share of TVU mass on late-adopter cohorts whose cohort-time effects have the same sign as the TWFE estimand but a larger magnitude --- so the forbidden later-vs-earlier comparisons in TWFE pull the aggregate \emph{toward} zero relative to a uniformly-weighted CS-DID. In each of these four cases the original paper's substantive conclusion is reinforced by modern reanalysis, not undermined; the published TWFE understates the effect rather than overstating it.

\subsection{Lesson 3. Dynamic reanalysis often changes the story about timing, persistence, and design credibility}

Dynamic specifications matter because they are the main tool for assessing timing, including whether effects appear immediately or with delay, whether they persist or fade, and whether the pre-treatment path supports the identifying assumptions. Figure~\ref{fig:ch3_eventstudy_panel} presents six cases that illustrate the main patterns revealed by dynamic reanalysis. The results are:

{\small
\begin{description}[style=unboxed,leftmargin=1.4em,labelsep=0.3em,font=\normalfont]
    \item[\textbf{\textcite{dranove2021dose}}:] TWFE, CS-NT, and CS-NYT trace nearly the same post-treatment decline. Confidence intervals widen at longer horizons, but the substantive interpretation remains unchanged.

    \item[\textbf{\textcite{carpenter2019direct}}:] The main difference comes from the control group. TWFE tracks CS-NT closely, while CS-NYT is systematically smaller in the post-treatment periods, suggesting a weaker effect under the broader comparison.

    \item[\textbf{\textcite{lawler2023effect}}:] The disagreement concerns persistence at longer horizons. All estimators show a positive short-run effect, but the CS profiles weaken at the end of the event window while TWFE remains more persistently positive.

    \item[\textbf{\textcite{cao2023mitigating}}:] The robust profile is substantially stronger. TWFE turns negative after treatment, but both CS estimators become much more negative at longer horizons, implying a larger long-run effect.

    \item[\textbf{\textcite{bosch2014tradeoffs}}:] The disagreement again concerns persistence. TWFE becomes increasingly negative over time, whereas the CS estimates show a smaller post-treatment decline that largely fades at longer horizons.

    \item[\textbf{\textcite{brodeur2025shootings}}:] The main message is diagnostic, but not because the estimators diverge sharply. In others, re-estimation changes the inferred persistence of the effect, the role of control-group choice, or the interpretation of the pre-treatment path. \subsection{Lesson 4. Dynamic contamination, long tails, and binning}
\label{sec:binning}

Long-horizon coefficients are the weakest part of a dynamic DiD design. It is worth separating two distinct problems that both worsen in the tails. \textit{Thin and compositionally changing support} affects every event-study estimator --- TWFE, Callaway--Sant'Anna, Sun--Abraham, and Borusyak--Jaravel--Spiess alike. At long horizons, the set of contributing cohorts shrinks, and the units that remain are not a random subsample; switching estimators does not recover the horizons that are missing from the data. \textit{Cross-horizon contamination}, by contrast, is specific to TWFE: each TWFE event-study coefficient is a weighted average of CATTs from all relative periods, not just the target horizon, and so heterogeneous effects at distant horizons leak into the implicit weights of the interior coefficients a reader actually reads. This second problem is exactly what modern estimators remove by constructing event-study paths from clean group-time averages; the first problem is a constraint of the data. Endpoint binning raises both problems at once. Pooling all periods below $-K_{\text{pre}}$ and above $K_{\text{post}}$ into far-pre and far-post indicators keeps the event study estimable, but it also changes the estimand. The far-post coefficient is no longer tied to a single horizon; it becomes a summary whose interpretation is shaped by cross-period contamination, implicit within-bin weighting, and compositional change across horizons.\footnote{For contamination in TWFE event studies, see \textcite{sun2021estimating}. For weighting distortions induced by binning, see \textcite{borusyak2023did_imputation}. For horizon-specific compositional change in staggered designs, see \textcite{callaway2021difference}. For compositional changes in repeated cross-sections, see \textcite{sant2026difference}.}

\subsubsection{An illustrative case: Lawler \& Yewell (2023)}

\textcite{lawler2023effect} study the effect of Baby-Friendly Hospital designation on breastfeeding initiation using a repeated cross-section of U.S.\ birth records, with county and birth-year fixed effects, an event window of $[-4,+4]$, and endpoint bins $F_5$ ($e \leq -5$) and $L_4$ ($e \geq 4$). This is a common applied specification when distant horizons are pooled into endpoint bins. Figure~\ref{fig:panel_binning_76} shows four diagnostics.

Panel~(a) shows that both TWFE and CS-NYT detect a positive post-treatment effect, but the TWFE profile is systematically larger, especially at the far-post endpoint. Panel~(b) shows that this long-run coefficient is sensitive to how distant horizons are handled. Replacing the binned TWFE with an unbinned version reduces the estimate at $t=+4$ from $+0.046$ to $+0.028$, while the CS-NYT estimate at the same horizon is smaller still. Panel~(c) shows why the endpoint coefficient is not really about $e=4$ alone. The Sun--Abraham decomposition assigns substantial weight to remote post-treatment horizons and non-zero weight to pre-treatment periods. Panel~(d) reaches the same conclusion from a different diagnostic. As the specification absorbs more distant periods into the far-post bin, the coefficient rises from about $+0.029$ to $+0.046$.

This case brings the three margins together in a single application. The far-post estimate is not a direct reading of persistence at one horizon. It reflects contamination, implicit within-bin weighting, and the changing composition of the observations that identify the tail. Even when the overall event study looks orderly, the tail is usually the weakest part of the profile.

\subsubsection{Prevalence across the sample}

\textcite{lawler2023effect} is not an isolated case. Table~\ref{tab:progbin_sensitivity} applies a progressive-binning exercise to 21 articles with dynamic event studies and sufficient variation in treatment timing. In the full sample, the median absolute change is 34\%, and 16 of 21 articles move by more than 20\%. Even among the 12 cases where the coefficient at $k=0$ is statistically significant, the median absolute change remains 22\%.

The recurring pattern is instability in the tail. Long-horizon coefficients often change under reasonable regroupings of distant periods. Endpoint bins remain a practical way to keep dynamic specifications estimable, but their coefficients should be read as weaker evidence on persistence than shorter-horizon estimates. In TWFE, the tail is where cross-period contamination and implicit within-bin weighting are often most severe. In heterogeneity-robust estimators, the analogous difficulty is event-time imbalance, because the set of contributing groups thins out as the horizon lengthens.

\subsection{Lesson 5. Robustness to heterogeneity does not imply robustness to parallel-trends violations}

Robustness to treatment-effect heterogeneity and robustness to violations of parallel trends are different issues. Replacing TWFE with an estimator that is robust to heterogeneous effects can change magnitudes, standard errors, and significance, but it does not repair a design built on a weak counterfactual path. A heterogeneity-robust estimator can improve the mapping between the estimate and the causal parameter, but it does not make the identifying counterfactual more credible. Table~\ref{tab:ch3_honestdid} shows that HonestDiD robustness remains limited under both estimators.

In both the first post-treatment period and the peak post-treatment coefficient, the median breakdown value is zero, and the share of articles with $\bar M = 0$ remains high. The exact values differ somewhat across TWFE and CS-DID, but the overall pattern is the same. Strong robustness to departures from exact parallel trends is uncommon in this sample, and switching estimators rarely changes that conclusion.\footnote{The breakdown value $\bar{M}_{\text{peak}}$ reported in Table~\ref{tab:ch3_honestdid} targets the peak event-study coefficient selected by the largest absolute $t$-statistic, not the aggregate ATT. When the conventional confidence interval of the aggregate already covers zero, the aggregate breakdown is trivially zero; the peak-coefficient breakdown can still be positive if individual event-time coefficients are individually significant. Readers comparing the per-article cards (Appendix~\ref{AppendixB}) should interpret $\bar{M}_{\text{peak}}$ as characterising the peak dynamic coefficient, whereas concordance between TWFE and CS-DID (Section~\ref{Chapter4}, Lesson~1) characterises the static aggregate.} This distinction is central to the design-based interpretation of DiD. Choosing a heterogeneity-robust estimator can improve the interpretability of the estimand, but it does not answer whether the identifying counterfactual is reliable. Recent work has emphasized this point directly. Estimator choice and design credibility are related, but separate, questions \parencite{baker2025difference}. In this sample, sensitivity to parallel-trends violations usually survives the move from TWFE to CS-DID.

\paragraph{Interpreting the TWFE column of Table~\ref{tab:ch3_honestdid}.} HonestDiD was developed by \textcite{rambachan2023more} under the assumption that the input event-study coefficients are unbiased estimates of the group-time causal effect at each horizon. In staggered-adoption designs this assumption is not satisfied by TWFE event-study coefficients: \textcite{sun2021estimating} show that each TWFE event-study coefficient is a weighted average of cohort-specific treatment effects at \emph{all} relative periods, not just the target horizon, so the pre-trend coefficients that HonestDiD takes as its null-hypothesis anchor are themselves contaminated by heterogeneous post-treatment effects. The TWFE column of Table~\ref{tab:ch3_honestdid} should therefore be read as \emph{the sensitivity bound an applied researcher would compute if they ran HonestDiD on the TWFE event study as-is, ignoring the Sun--Abraham contamination}. It is the naive-interpretation column, not a causal benchmark. The CS-DID column, by contrast, feeds HonestDiD with group-time ATTs whose pre-treatment coefficients are uncontaminated by construction, and is the one whose $\bar M$ values admit the standard Rambachan--Roth interpretation. That the two columns produce qualitatively similar breakdown distributions --- median $\bar M = 0$ at both first and peak horizons, with the $\bar M = 0$ share moving between 52\% and 75\% across cells --- is the substantive finding: the design fragility diagnosed by HonestDiD does not disappear when the event-study inputs are cleaned of TWFE's weighting issues.

\subsection{Lesson 6. In staggered designs, the control group is part of the identification strategy}

In staggered-adoption designs, the counterfactual is not fixed. Choosing between \emph{never-treated} (NT) and \emph{not-yet-treated} (NYT) changes the comparison group and the identifying parallel-trends assumption. This choice is part of the design itself. As \textcite{baker2025difference} emphasize, the same group--time effect can be constructed with either control group, but the two approaches rely on different untreated outcome paths. The key question is which path is more credible in the application.

Figure~\ref{fig:nt_nyt_examples} shows this clearly. In some applications, NT and NYT rely on similar comparisons and generate nearly identical dynamic profiles. In others, the never-treated group is thin and the NYT pool shrinks quickly as treatment spreads, so the two estimators rely on visibly different counterfactuals. The divergence in those cases comes from identification. Similar estimates under NT and NYT are reassuring, but they do not make the control-group choice irrelevant. Different estimates show that the design is being identified from different counterfactual strategies. In staggered designs, the control group is part of the design because it determines which parallel-trends assumption the reader is being asked to accept.

\subsection{Lesson 7. Covariates in DiD belong to the design}

Many original articles estimate TWFE with covariates or additional fixed effects, while the corresponding CS-DID replication is estimated unconditionally. Concretely, among the 53 articles in the comparable subsample, 36 include covariates or additional fixed effects in the original TWFE specification, and only 3 of those 36 are re-estimated with covariates in CS-DID; in the remaining cases, the CS-DID replication is unconditional. This is a protocol difference, not only an estimator difference, and its practical consequences are examined throughout this section. To isolate the estimator from the covariate margin, we also run a matched-protocol CS-DID with the paper's original controls as \texttt{xformla}, wrapped in error handling so that papers with highly collinear covariate sets gracefully produce a documented failure rather than a silent substitution. Table~\ref{tab:ch3_aggregate_concordance} reports both the unconditional and the matched-protocol comparisons where the latter is estimable. Figure~\ref{fig:density_covariates} summarizes the comparison between the original controlled TWFE estimates and the unconditional CS-DID replications. The correlation between the scaled coefficients is high, and the median ratio of point estimates is close to one. This comparison shows how much the original controls were changing the identifying comparison. In much of the sample, removing them in the modern replication leaves the estimate largely unchanged.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.9\textwidth]{Figures/agregado/density_covariates_zscore.pdf}
\caption{Density of scaled coefficients (estimate\,/\,TWFE SE) for articles whose original TWFE specification includes covariates or additional fixed effects. Grey: original TWFE with controls; blue: unconditional CS-DID replication.}
\label{fig:density_covariates}
\end{figure}

In DiD, covariates play two distinct roles. They belong to the design when they help justify \emph{conditional} parallel trends, and they can improve precision even when the unconditional assumption already holds: the doubly robust estimator of \textcite{sant2020doubly} --- which \textcite{callaway2021difference} adopts --- delivers efficiency gains from covariates without requiring them for identification. When covariates are used, they should be tied either to a conditional parallel-trends argument or to a documented efficiency gain, and the two motivations have different implications for how their removal should be assessed. A stable point estimate after dropping covariates is consistent with both: under an identification motivation, it suggests the covariates were not needed to support the comparison; under an efficiency motivation, it is exactly what the theory predicts, with standard errors carrying the cost of the removal. Reporting the standard-error ratio alongside the point-estimate ratio --- as we do in the per-article cards of Appendix~\ref{AppendixB} --- allows readers to distinguish the two cases.

An important distinction is between baseline and time-varying covariates. Baseline covariates can strengthen the design when untreated trends differ systematically across observable groups and conditioning on pre-treatment characteristics makes the comparison more credible. Time-varying covariates are different. Once they enter the specification, the identifying comparison is conditioned on variables that evolve over time and may themselves be related to treatment or to its mechanisms, which can complicate identification \parencite{caetano_callaway2024_hiddenlinearity,caetano2022difference}.

\subsubsection{Decomposing the TWFE vs CS-DID gap: estimator versus covariates}
\label{sec:matched_protocol}

A natural concern with the comparison above is that it confounds two margins at once: the estimator swap (TWFE $\rightarrow$ CS-DID) and the covariate margin (with $X$ $\rightarrow$ without $X$). To isolate these, for each of the papers whose original TWFE specification includes covariates, we also run a matched-protocol CS-DID in which the \texttt{xformla} argument is set to the same covariate set as the original paper and the doubly-robust estimator of \textcite{sant2020doubly} is used. This produces three parallel specifications: (A) both TWFE and CS-DID with controls, (B) both without, and (C) the common paper-vs-replication comparison, with TWFE using the paper's controls and CS-DID unconditional.\footnote{A non-trivial number of papers fail specification~(A) when the covariate set is highly collinear, too numerous for the doubly-robust propensity-score estimator to fit without overfit, or too large to fit in memory; the template records these as \texttt{FAIL\_collinear}, \texttt{status=OK, ATT=0} (silent numerical collapse), or \texttt{FAIL\_memory} rather than silently substituting in a different specification. Out of the 53-paper comparable subsample, 32 produce an estimable matched-protocol estimate (Figure~\ref{fig:ch3_aggregate_scatter_matched}). The 21 papers where Spec~A failed form a substantive class of findings in their own right, discussed below.}

Table~\ref{tab:ch3_aggregate_concordance} compares the three specifications on the subsample where all three are available. Two empirical facts are worth flagging. \textit{First}, the covariate margin is not second-order: the median absolute proportional shift in the point estimate between specifications (B) and (C) --- holding the estimator fixed at TWFE-with-controls on one side, and changing the CS-DID side from unconditional to matched --- is substantially different from the median under (C) alone. The matched-protocol CS-DID systematically shifts further from TWFE than the unconditional CS-DID does in this sample, not closer. \textit{Second}, the direction of this shift is ambiguous in the aggregate: for some papers the matched-protocol estimate is closer to the TWFE point; for others it is further away. The aggregate picture is therefore not a simple ``controls explain the gap'' story. The gap between TWFE-with-controls and CS-DID is split across an estimator margin \emph{and} a covariate margin, and the two do not always point in the same direction.

A useful interpretation draws on \textcite{caetano2022difference} and \textcite{caetano_callaway2024_hiddenlinearity}. Under TWFE with time-varying covariates, the regression implicitly conditions on within-unit changes in $X$ after the unit fixed effect is absorbed, and its target estimand is not the same object as the $ATT(g,t)$ recovered by CS-DID with an $X$-conditional propensity-score model. Even a protocol-matched comparison, therefore, compares two estimators that target slightly different conditional-trend assumptions; the matched-protocol exercise narrows the estimand gap but does not eliminate it. The practical implication for readers of the original papers is that the divergence between TWFE and modern estimators is not reducible to a single ``fix,'' and covariate-handling choices require explicit justification --- either as baseline conditioning under an identification argument, or as efficiency-only additions under \textcite{sant2020doubly}.

\subsection{Lesson 8. Geographic conditioning opens a different design margin in CS-DID}

Geographic conditioning creates a distinct source of divergence between TWFE and CS-DID. In the usual TWFE specification with unit and time fixed effects, time-invariant geographic dummies are absorbed by the unit effects. Researchers who want to account for geographic heterogeneity usually rely on interacted fixed effects, such as state-by-time or region-by-time terms. In CS-DID, geography can enter directly as a covariate in the nuisance models. This makes geographic conditioning a more explicit design choice.

CS-DID makes this easier, but it can also create new problems. In the doubly robust estimator of \citet{sant2020doubly}, these controls enter the propensity score model. When geography is finely partitioned and some cells contain few treated or control observations, the estimator can become unstable. The relevant empirical question is whether the estimate still changes once overlap is restored.

Figure~\ref{fig:graduated_sensitivity} implements a graduated sensitivity exercise. It starts from baseline conditioning, then adds coarser and finer geographic controls, and finally re-estimates the state-conditioned specification after restricting the sample to states with minimum treated and control support. Four patterns emerge. Some cases show attenuation that remains after the overlap restriction, which is consistent with geography absorbing relevant confounding variation. Others collapse under full state conditioning and return once weak-support states are removed; we interpret this pattern as suggestive of overfitting, but acknowledge an alternative reading in which the restriction changes the target estimand rather than the estimation quality. Dropping states with thin treated or control support narrows the population of interest, and if treatment effects vary geographically --- the premise of heterogeneous-effects designs throughout this literature --- the restricted-sample ATT differs from the full-sample ATT by construction. We treat these cases conservatively: when the dropped states differ systematically from the retained states on observable characteristics, the ``return'' to the baseline estimate may reflect selection into a particular geographic subset rather than a pure overfitting correction. A third group remains stable across specifications, suggesting that geographic conditioning is not first-order. A fourth group becomes hard to interpret because the overlap-restricted sample is too selective.

Geographic conditioning is informative when there is a clear geographic identification concern or when the original design already relies on differential geographic trends, but only when support remains credible. Otherwise, a richer conditioning set can produce an estimate that is less stable and less informative than the baseline result.

\subsection{Lesson 9. Aggregation choice in CS-DID can change the result}
In CS-DID, the comparison with TWFE depends not only on the underlying \(ATT(g,t)\), but also on how those effects are aggregated.\footnote{The three main aggregation schemes are group, simple, and dynamic. The first averages within cohorts and then across cohorts. The second averages directly across all group-time cells. The third averages across cohorts at each event-time horizon and then collapses the post-treatment profile into a single effect.} Table~\ref{tab:ch3_aggte_comparison} shows that this choice matters empirically. Across the 52 articles available under all three rules, 12 change classification when the aggregation rule changes. Dynamic aggregation yields the highest concordance with TWFE, while group and simple aggregation generate more reversals and more cases of divergence. 

Changing the aggregation rule changes the empirical object being compared with TWFE. In some applications, the effect is small. In others, it is enough to alter the substantive classification of the result. Therefore, aggregation is part of the estimand, and a CS-DID estimate is not fully defined unless the aggregation rule is stated.

\subsubsection{Joint margin-attribution across the five divergence margins}
\label{sec:margin_attribution}

The preceding lessons identified five distinct margins at which TWFE and CS-DID can disagree: covariate handling (Lesson~7), control-group choice (Lesson~6), dynamic aggregation (Lesson~9), endpoint binning (Lesson~4), and software balancing defaults (Lesson~10). Each lesson reports the margin in isolation. A natural follow-up question, raised directly by our peer-reviewed methods audit, is whether the margins are additive (each contributes a roughly-independent share of the headline gap) or interactive (the joint movement differs from the sum of the marginal movements). Table~\ref{tab:margin_attribution} puts the margins on a common scale --- absolute proportional gap $|\Delta| = |a - b| / |\hat\beta^{\text{TWFE}}_{+X}|$ --- and reports the across-paper distribution of each.

\input{Tables/table_4_7_margin_attribution.tex}

Three facts stand out. \textit{First}, the \textbf{headline protocol gap (Spec~C)} has a median of 21.8\% across the 49 papers where it is estimable: half the papers sit inside a 22\% band, and three quarters sit inside 61\%. The aggregate TWFE-vs-CS divergence is therefore substantial in the tails but modest at the median. \textit{Second}, the \textbf{estimator margin dominates the covariate margin on the TWFE side} (median 24.1\% under Spec~B vs.\ 9.7\% for the TWFE-side covariate margin). This is the clean test of the ``estimator change vs covariate change'' decomposition that Referee~B demanded: when both sides are unconditional (Spec~B), the estimator swap alone produces a larger typical movement than the covariate ablation does when the estimator is held at TWFE. \textit{Third}, the \textbf{aggregation} (median 0.0\%) and \textbf{control-group} (median 2.7\%) margins are tight in the center of the distribution, with only the tails contributing to divergence. Binning is the outlier: Lesson~4 reports a median $|\Delta| = 34\%$ on a smaller subsample (21 papers), driven by compositional changes in the far-post bin rather than by the estimator swap. Read together, these numbers justify the sequencing in the checklist of Section~\ref{sec:applied_guide}: covariate justification and binning sensitivity are prerequisites for reporting a single CS-DID number, the estimator swap itself is second-order by median but important in the tails, and the aggregation/control-group choices are the margins on which the typical paper is least sensitive.

The sizable mean-vs-median gap in every row reflects a small number of high-leverage tail papers. The Spec~A estimator margin in particular has a mean of 631\% that is pulled by two papers where the matched-protocol CS-DID inflates relative to TWFE by an order of magnitude; these are the ``inflate'' and ``collapse'' cases documented in the hexagon taxonomy (Table~\ref{tab:lesson7_hexagon}). Users who want to guard against such tail outcomes should consult the per-article cards in Appendix~\ref{AppendixB} rather than the aggregate median alone.

\subsection{Lesson 10. Software implementation can change the result}
\label{sec:li_sensitivity}

Software implementation is another source of divergence in applied DiD because different implementations can imply different effective samples when the panel is incomplete. In R, researchers can impose full balancing, keeping only units observed in all periods, or allow an unbalanced panel and use all available observations. Stata's \texttt{csdid2}, by contrast, applies a pair-balanced rule, retaining units observed in the base period and in the current period for each \(\mathrm{ATT}(g,t)\).\footnote{One other mechanical difference can be neutralized by explicit option choices. R defaults to a varying base period ($t-1$), whereas Stata defaults to a universal one ($g-1$). The reanalysis standardized all estimates to a universal base period.} These procedures coincide in complete panels, but not when missingness is substantial.

Figure~\ref{fig:sensitivity_210} shows the consequence in \textcite{li2025shortened}. The fully balanced R specification and the pair-balanced Stata implementation both produce positive post-treatment effects. The unbalanced R specification, however, produces a mostly negative post-treatment profile and a visible pre-treatment dip. In this case, the balancing rule changes the qualitative conclusion. Implementation choices should be treated as part of the empirical design. When missingness is nontrivial, researchers should report the fill rate and the balancing rule, because both affect the sample behind the estimate.

\paragraph{The balancing rule used in this paper.} The R template of this reanalysis calls \texttt{did::att\_gt} with the metadata flag \texttt{allow\_unbalanced\_panel} set per article. When the flag is \texttt{FALSE} (the default in the original \texttt{did} package), the estimator uses its internal pair-balanced rule over each \((g,t)\) comparison --- analogous to Stata's \texttt{csdid2} rather than to full-sample balancing. When the flag is set to \texttt{TRUE} for an article whose original specification allowed unbalanced estimation, we pass all available observations and let \texttt{att\_gt} honour its unbalanced code path. Metadata fields \texttt{panel\_setup.allow\_unbalanced} and \texttt{panel\_setup.cs\_allow\_unbalanced} document this decision per article; the fill rate of each panel article is reported in the per-article cards of Appendix~\ref{AppendixB}. Balancing choice is consequential only for articles with non-trivial missingness, most visibly \textcite{li2025shortened} shown in Figure~\ref{fig:sensitivity_210}.

\paragraph{Empirical sweep: the consequence of the default.} An April 2026 audit ran the full pipeline on the 22 articles in the sample that had \texttt{allow\_unbalanced=TRUE} as their initial setting, toggling each one to \texttt{FALSE} and re-estimating. The results split cleanly. For 17 articles the CS-DID estimates were essentially unchanged at TWFE byte-identical, indicating that the originally declared unbalanced flag was not in fact consequential (the panels were either balanced or near-balanced). For two articles --- \textcite{sarmiento2023low} and \textcite{fisman2017distortionary} --- the CS-NT estimate moved by 41\% in magnitude (sign preserved); both were retained at \texttt{FALSE} because the more conservative pair-balanced behaviour is the modern default. For three articles --- \textcite{greenstone2014environmental}, \textcite{bhalotra2022maternal}, and \textcite{hausman2014corporate} --- CS-NT returned \texttt{NA} under \texttt{FALSE} because \texttt{did\_standarization} dropped enough units to balance that the remaining cohorts were too small to estimate; for these the flag was reverted to \texttt{TRUE} with the technical reason logged. The qualitative implication for applied work is that \texttt{allow\_unbalanced=FALSE} is the right default but cannot be applied universally: researchers should report the panel fill rate, run both settings as a sensitivity check, and reserve \texttt{TRUE} for cases where the balanced subsample is too small for the modern estimator to operate.

\paragraph{Five CS-DID recoveries via metadata-only fixes.} Beyond the sweep above, the iterative-review phase (Appendix~\ref{AppendixC}, §\ref{sec:iterative_review}) recovered five articles whose CS-DID had previously returned \texttt{NA} or numerical collapse, through metadata edits alone --- no template modifications. \textcite{soliman2025disrupting} was upgraded from a 7.7\% WARN to an EXACT match on TWFE by adding \texttt{cs\_sample\_filter=""} (CS-DID skips the rel-year window the paper applies to TWFE only) and \texttt{cs\_min\_e=-3, cs\_max\_e=3} (the dynamic aggregation is then clipped to the paper's reported window). \textcite{arbogast2022administrative}, \textcite{johnson2024legal}, and \textcite{galasso2024licensing} all recovered from CS-DID \texttt{NA} via the \texttt{allow\_unbalanced} toggle described above. \textcite{maclean2025effects} recovered after removing a state-by-year pre-aggregation step from the template; CS-DID now runs directly on the 78,000 monthly individual-level RCS observations and produces a CS-NT estimate that, while it still sign-reverses against the TWFE-with-controls headline, does so on the same data the paper actually uses. These are not improvements on the paper's identification; they are improvements on the reanalysis's faithfulness to the paper's own data structure.

\subsection{Toward an applied guide to DiD}
\label{sec:applied_guide}

The ten lessons above point to a set of checks that an applied DiD paper should pass before the estimate is read as a causal effect. \textcite{baker2025difference} provide a forward-engineering framework --- from target parameter to identifying assumptions to matched estimator to robustness --- that we take as the structural backdrop. Our contribution here is narrower and complementary: a pre-submission rubric, calibrated to the patterns documented in Sections~\ref{sec:binning}--\ref{sec:li_sensitivity}, that a reader or referee can apply in one page. Each item is anchored to a specific section, table, or figure of this paper.

\begin{tcolorbox}[
  colback=gray!5,
  colframe=black!70,
  arc=1mm,
  boxrule=0.5pt,
  left=6pt,right=6pt,top=6pt,bottom=6pt,
  title={\textbf{Box 4.1. Pre-Submission DiD Checklist}},
  fonttitle=\small\bfseries,
  coltitle=black,
  colbacktitle=gray!15
]
\footnotesize

\textbf{A. Estimand and identification}

\vspace{0.3em}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.6em,itemsep=0.25em,label=\arabic*.]
\item Is the target estimand stated \emph{before} the regression? (ATT(g,t), dynamic ATT, simple/group/dynamic aggregate.) \textit{See Lesson~9 / Table~\ref{tab:ch3_aggte_comparison}: aggregation choice changes classification in 12 of 52 papers.}

\item If staggered: is the control-group choice (NT vs.\ NYT) justified on identification grounds? \textit{See Lesson~6 / Figure~\ref{fig:nt_nyt_examples}: \textcite{carpenter2019direct} shows how a thin never-treated pool can materially shift the comparison.}

\item If conditional parallel trends: are baseline and time-varying covariates distinguished, with each justified on the corresponding assumption? \textit{See Lesson~7 / \textcite{caetano2022difference}; \textcite{hoynes2015income} is an illustrative case where covariates re-enter matters for both pre-trends and the post-treatment estimate.}
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{B. Dynamic specification}

\vspace{0.3em}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.6em,itemsep=0.25em,label=\arabic*.,start=4]
\item Are the event-time window, the reference period, and the endpoint-bin structure reported explicitly?

\item Has the far-post coefficient been checked under progressive binning? \textit{Rule: if $|\Delta| > 20\%$ between the terminal-horizon and all-horizons versions, the far-post bin is a compositional summary rather than a horizon-specific effect and should be interpreted as such. See Lesson~4 / Table~\ref{tab:progbin_sensitivity}: this threshold fails in 16 of 21 papers.}

\item For the binned endpoint: are Sun--Abraham weights reported so that contamination of interior coefficients is visible? \textit{See Lesson~4 / Figure~\ref{fig:panel_binning_76}(c), which shows substantial weight on horizons far beyond the labelled $k$ for the endpoint coefficient.}
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{C. Robustness and parallel-trends sensitivity}

\vspace{0.3em}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.6em,itemsep=0.25em,label=\arabic*.,start=7]
\item Has HonestDiD been applied to both the first post-treatment period and the peak dynamic coefficient? \textit{See Lesson~5 / Table~\ref{tab:ch3_honestdid}: the median $\bar M$ is zero at the first period in the majority of papers under both estimators.}

\item Has CS-DID been cross-checked under both never-treated and not-yet-treated controls? \textit{See Lesson~6.}

\item For staggered designs: has the Bacon decomposition's treated-versus-treated share been reported? \textit{Rule: if TvT share exceeds 50\%, the headline TWFE coefficient is constructed largely from comparisons that are not clean causal contrasts and a modern estimator should be the primary specification. See Lesson~2 / Table~\ref{tab:bacon_summary}: this threshold is exceeded in 8 of 26 staggered articles.}
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{D. Implementation and effective sample}

\vspace{0.3em}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.6em,itemsep=0.25em,label=\arabic*.,start=10]
\item Is the panel balancing rule stated (full balanced, unbalanced, or pair-balanced)? \textit{See Lesson~10 / Figure~\ref{fig:sensitivity_210}: three balancing rules yield qualitatively different profiles in \textcite{li2025shortened}.}

\item Is the base-period convention (varying $t-1$ vs.\ universal $g-1$) documented? \textit{See Lesson~10.}

\item Is the fill rate reported for each panel article with non-trivial missingness?
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{E. Honest framing and comparability}

\vspace{0.3em}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.6em,itemsep=0.25em,label=\arabic*.,start=13]
\item Is the negative-weights diagnostic \emph{reported} (share, Bacon decomposition) rather than \emph{interpreted} as a verdict on TWFE validity? \textit{Rule: a share above roughly 15--20\% is worth flagging; below about 5\% the diagnostic is rarely first-order. See Lesson~2: our staggered sample has mean 5.8\%, median 1.1\%, maximum 23.8\%.}

\item Is robustness to treatment-effect heterogeneity (estimator choice) distinguished from robustness to parallel-trends violations (HonestDiD)? \textit{See Lesson~5: the two are different objects; switching to a heterogeneity-robust estimator does not imply robustness to trend violations.}

\item Are the design-margin assumptions (control group, covariate set, aggregation rule, balancing rule, base period) visible to the reader, or are they hidden inside software defaults? \textit{See Lesson~10.}
\end{enumerate}
\end{tcolorbox}

Box~4.1 is not a substitute for the design reasoning in \textcite{baker2025difference} or in the methodological references cited throughout this chapter. Its purpose is operational: a reviewer or applied researcher can scan the 15 items and identify which empirical margin the paper has left implicit. Our reanalysis shows that papers disagree with modern re-estimation most often on the margins this box foregrounds, and that failure to surface these margins is the commonest source of reader difficulty in the sample.

Two companion artefacts accompany the checklist. Box~\ref{box:decision_tree} is a decision tree for selecting a primary modern estimator given the design features that determine estimator availability and plausibility. Box~\ref{box:failure_catalog} is a failure-mode catalog summarising eleven implementation traps that the AI-assisted reanalysis pipeline recurrently encountered while re-estimating the 56 papers (53 of which form the comparable subsample); each item is a specific mistake we would not have identified without running the pipeline end-to-end, and each has cost researcher time elsewhere in the published literature. The full catalog has 51 entries as of April 2026 (Appendix~\ref{AppendixC}).

\footnotesize
The tree below orders the design questions that determine which modern estimator is available and primary. Work through the branches top-to-bottom; the rightmost label gives the recommended primary specification. Every path presumes the article reports an event study whose reference period and window are documented.

\vspace{0.4em}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.6em,itemsep=0.25em,label=Q\arabic*.]
\item \textbf{Is the data panel or repeated cross-section (RCS)?}
  \begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.2em,itemsep=0.1em]
    \item \emph{Panel} $\rightarrow$ go to Q2.
    \item \emph{RCS} $\rightarrow$ CS-DID with \texttt{panel=FALSE} and a synthetic row-id (Lesson~10); \textcite{borusyak2024revisiting} imputation is generally not available without reconstructing a pseudo-panel.
  \end{itemize}
\item \textbf{Is treatment timing single or staggered?}
  \begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.2em,itemsep=0.1em]
    \item \emph{Single-cohort} $\rightarrow$ CS-DID, SA, Gardner, and BJS all coincide up to aggregation; TWFE is unbiased under homogeneous effects. Primary: CS-DID (simple aggregate) as a check on TWFE; Bacon decomposition is mechanically degenerate.
    \item \emph{Staggered} $\rightarrow$ go to Q3.
  \end{itemize}
\item \textbf{Is the never-treated (NT) pool large (say, at least 15--20\% of units)?}
  \begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.2em,itemsep=0.1em]
    \item \emph{Yes} $\rightarrow$ CS-NT is the natural primary; CS-NYT as a robustness check.
    \item \emph{No} (thin or absent NT pool) $\rightarrow$ CS-NYT is the primary (see \textcite{marcus2021role} on the associated trade-off); CS-NT if any NT units remain is a robustness check only.
  \end{itemize}
\item \textbf{Does the original paper include covariates that plausibly affect pre-trends?}
  \begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.2em,itemsep=0.1em]
    \item \emph{Yes, pre-treatment / baseline covariates} $\rightarrow$ CS-DID with \texttt{xformla = \textasciitilde{}X}, doubly-robust estimator \parencite{sant2020doubly}.
    \item \emph{Yes, time-varying covariates potentially endogenous to treatment} $\rightarrow$ drop them or report both conditional and unconditional (see Lesson~7 / \textcite{caetano2022difference}).
    \item \emph{No} $\rightarrow$ unconditional CS-DID is the primary; efficiency gains from \textcite{sant2020doubly} do not apply.
  \end{itemize}
\item \textbf{Does the event study bin its endpoints?}
  \begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.2em,itemsep=0.1em]
    \item \emph{Yes} $\rightarrow$ report the progressive-binning sensitivity (Box~4.1 item~5). If $|\Delta| > 20\%$, treat the far-post coefficient as a compositional summary, not a horizon-specific effect.
    \item \emph{No} $\rightarrow$ report the widest estimable horizon window; document which horizons are identified by which cohorts.
  \end{itemize}
\item \textbf{Does the design rely on a specific balancing rule under missingness?}
  \begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.2em,itemsep=0.1em]
    \item \emph{Fill rate $\geq 95\%$} $\rightarrow$ any rule is fine; state the choice.
    \item \emph{Fill rate $< 95\%$} $\rightarrow$ compare full-balanced (R), unbalanced (R), and pair-balanced (Stata's \texttt{csdid2}) specifications; Lesson~10 documents how balancing rule can flip the qualitative conclusion.
  \end{itemize}
\end{enumerate}

  colframe=black!70,

  label={box:failure_catalog}

\footnotesize
The full catalog of implementation patterns is in Appendix~\ref{AppendixC}. The eleven below are the traps most worth flagging to an applied user of modern DiD tools; each was encountered during reanalysis of a specific paper in the sample and each produced a silently wrong number until identified.

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.6em,itemsep=0.25em,label=\arabic*.]
\item \textbf{Silent CS/TWFE sample mismatch} (\textcite{kresch2020buck}). If the CS-DID replication runs on a different sample than TWFE (because the original paper's sample filter was not applied to the CS branch), the resulting comparison is not an estimator swap but a protocol swap. Always assert \texttt{n\_distinct(df\_cs[[idname]]) == n\_distinct(df[[idname]])}.

\item \textbf{Late-treated units contaminating the never-treated pool} (Pattern~25). In \texttt{did::att\_gt}, any \texttt{gname > max(tname)} is silently coerced to \texttt{Inf} (never-treated), inflating the NT comparison group with units that will be treated later. Drop them explicitly before calling \texttt{att\_gt} with \texttt{control\_group="nevertreated"}.

\item \textbf{Universal vs varying base period produces different dynamic profiles} (Pattern~26). R's \texttt{did} defaults to a varying base period ($t-1$); Stata's \texttt{csdid2} to a universal base period ($g-1$). On balanced panels with perfect coverage, the two conventions can produce near-zero ATTs for one and non-zero for the other. Always set \texttt{base\_period = "universal"} to match the dominant convention in the modern DiD literature.

\item \textbf{BJS silently drops pre-period coefficients} (Pattern~3). The \texttt{did\_imputation} R interface requires negative horizons to be passed explicitly; without them, BJS returns only post-treatment $\tau_\ell$ and the researcher may falsely interpret the absence of pre-periods as evidence of no pre-trends.

\item \textbf{Dynamic aggregation polluted by long-horizon noise} (Pattern~48). \texttt{aggte(type="dynamic")} without \texttt{min\_e}/\texttt{max\_e} averages over all identifiable event-times, including horizons with only one or two contributing cohorts. For papers that display a narrow event window (e.g.\ $[-5, +5]$) but have data at longer horizons, the overall-dynamic ATT can be dominated by the noisy tail. Decide clipping explicitly based on the paper's displayed window.

\item \textbf{Cohort construction via pre-period dummy instead of treatment-onset dummy} (Pattern~14). If the original Stata code builds treatment cohorts from an anticipation dummy (e.g.\ \texttt{F1==1}) instead of the onset dummy (\texttt{L0==1}), the R port misaligns the event-time zero and shifts the event study by one period. Always validate \texttt{gvar} construction against a small sample of known-cohort units.

\item \textbf{Endpoint bin columns renamed to the paper's window} (Pattern~13). When original code collapses columns \texttt{L10, L11, \dots} into a single \texttt{L9} bin, the aggregate indicator \texttt{Post\_avg = any(L0 \dots L9) == 1} now captures a smaller horizon window than the variable name suggests, inflating the static ATT. Reconstruct \texttt{Post\_avg} from the paper's original L-definitions, not from variable names.

\item \textbf{Stata date formats converted as integers} (Patterns~7, 12). Stata's half-year (\texttt{th2012h2}) and monthly (\texttt{tm2010m10}) encodings come through \texttt{haven::read\_dta} as raw integers. If the R script treats them as years or quarters, cohort timing is off by orders of magnitude. Always inspect the Stata format attribute of date columns after import.

\item \textbf{Propensity-score overfitting with fine geographic dummies in \texttt{xformla}} (Pattern~42). Passing state or region dummies through \texttt{xformla} in the doubly-robust \texttt{att\_gt} call can make the propensity-score model numerically unstable when some geographic cells contain few treated or control observations. Collapse to coarser geography, restrict to overlap-supported cells, or drop the dummies and absorb them via additional fixed effects in the TWFE comparison instead.

\item \textbf{Non-deterministic standard errors from unset bootstrap seed} (Pattern~46). \texttt{did::att\_gt} uses multiplier bootstrap for standard errors. Without \texttt{set.seed(\cdot)}, reruns produce slightly different SEs and --- via the influence-function reweighting --- occasionally different point estimates. Always fix the seed at the top of the estimation script.

\item \textbf{RCS control collinearity silently inflating CS-DID ATT to false positive} (Pattern~51, documented April 2026, \textcite{levine2011effective}). When the CS-DID Spec~A is run on a repeated-cross-section paper with time-varying individual-level controls (e.g., \texttt{married}, \texttt{female}, demographic composition), the doubly-robust propensity-score model estimates IPW weights from individual covariates that vary across observations within the same unit-time cell. Unlike Pattern~42 (where the model fails to converge and \texttt{ATT} collapses to zero), the model converges with extreme but finite weights. The aggregation step then mechanically inflates the ATT by 3--10$\times$ relative to the no-controls CS-DID, often flipping a statistically null result into a false positive. TWFE is unaffected because OLS handles collinearity through the Frisch-Waugh projection without reweighting. Detection rule: when \texttt{data\_structure == "rcs"} and \texttt{|ATT\_A / ATT\_B| > 2} with a sign flip or significance change, flag as RCS control-collinearity inflation and treat the no-controls CS-DID (Spec~B) as the primary estimate.
\end{enumerate}
\end{tcolorbox}

The applied lesson of the reanalysis is narrower than a general design framework, but it is also closer to what applied work actually needs. Papers become easier to interpret when they report the estimand before the coefficient, treat the event study as a design object rather than a ritual, separate robustness to heterogeneity from robustness to parallel-trends violations, justify covariates on identification or efficiency grounds, and state implementation choices (aggregation, balancing, software defaults) explicitly. The key question is whether the empirical implementation keeps the causal target, the identifying comparison, and the available support aligned. If the causal target, the identifying comparison, and the available support are not aligned, using a different estimator will not fix the underlying design problem.


\section{Conclusion}
\label{Chapter5}

This paper reassessed 56 \emph{health economics} studies under a common replication and re-estimation protocol to examine how results change when TWFE is replaced by modern DiD estimators. The literature remains broadly recognizable, but many conclusions become less secure. In this sample, changes rarely take the form of sign reversal. They appear more often as changes in magnitude, weaker dynamic profiles, loss of precision, and greater sensitivity to design choices than the original articles suggested.

The contribution of this paper is empirical and comparative. Re-estimating a defined applied literature under a common protocol makes it possible to distinguish between results that remain substantively stable and results that weaken once identification choices are made explicit. It also sharpens how the recent critique of TWFE should be read in practice. In this sample, divergence is not driven mainly by negative weights. It more often reflects differences in control-group choice, support, dynamic aggregation, and the treatment of covariates. For applied work, the relevant question is whether the article's conclusion survives once those margins are made explicit under alternative estimators.

The practical output of the reanalysis is condensed into three artefacts at the end of Section~\ref{Chapter4}. Box~4.1 (Section~\ref{sec:applied_guide}) is a fifteen-item pre-submission checklist whose items are anchored to specific empirical patterns in the sample, with quantitative thresholds wherever the data support them. Box~\ref{box:decision_tree} is a decision tree for selecting a primary modern DiD estimator as a function of design features (panel vs.\ RCS, single vs.\ staggered, never-treated pool size, covariate set, endpoint-bin structure, fill rate). Box~\ref{box:failure_catalog} is a ten-item failure-mode catalog drawn from the resolution patterns the AI-assisted pipeline accumulated while re-estimating the 56 articles. A reader who wants the operational takeaway of the paper can photocopy those three pages and treat the rest of the manuscript as the evidence that calibrates them.

The exercise has clear limits. The sample is restricted to studies with usable replication materials and does not represent the full DiD literature. Applied on the comparable 53-article subsample (56 reanalyzed minus three structural exclusions), the headline sign-preservation rate is $47/53=88.7\%$; the selection-bounds exercise in Section~\ref{sec:selection_bounds} shows that under monotone-selection assumptions the population rate is bounded above by this number and can be much lower depending on the assumed reliability of the 1,831 excluded articles. The analysis uses the data and code environments provided by the original authors and may therefore inherit limitations from those replication packages. The sample is concentrated in \emph{health economics}, with many studies set in the United States, which limits generalization across fields and institutional settings. Some replications also required adaptations to make the original specifications compatible with a standardized implementation in \emph{R}. The scope of this paper is therefore specific. It provides an auditable and comparable reassessment of a defined sample.

These limits also point to natural extensions. The same protocol can be applied to literatures outside \emph{health economics}, to settings with different data structures, and to designs outside the present sample, including \emph{triple-DiD} and non-absorbing treatments.
